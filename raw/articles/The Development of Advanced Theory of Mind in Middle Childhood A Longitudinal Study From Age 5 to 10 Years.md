## Abstract

This 5-year longitudinal study investigated advanced theory-of-mind (AToM) development in 161 German 5- to 10-year-olds (89 females, 72 males). Core aspects of AToM developed nonlinearly, with children reaching a milestone at the age of 7 years, around when they attained the conceptual insight that mental states can be recursive. In late elementary school, a multicomponent battery was used. Performance on many aspects of AToM was predicted by information-processing skills (intelligence and language at 6 years), but not by the age when children acquired the basic conceptual insight; only some naturalistic, social-interpretative tasks were correlated with children’s age at acquisition. This study documents significant developmental progressions in middle-childhood AToM and suggests that different mechanisms may underlie diverse aspects of social cognition.

There is a growing consensus that theory-of-mind (ToM) development is not confined to the preschool years, with children acquiring an increasingly complex understanding of mental life throughout elementary school (Hughes & Devine, [2015](#cdev13627-bib-0028); Miller, [2009](#cdev13627-bib-0039)). In particular, children begin to acquire more-complex forms of false-belief understanding (Perner & Wimmer, [1985](#cdev13627-bib-0046)), they become more proficient in interpreting complex social situations (Baron-Cohen, O’Riordan, Stone, Jones, & Plaisted, [1999](#cdev13627-bib-0004); Bosacki & Astington, [1999](#cdev13627-bib-0009); Devine & Hughes, [2013](#cdev13627-bib-0015); Happé, [1994](#cdev13627-bib-0024)), and they improve their ability to interpret mental states and emotions from facial expressions (Baron-Cohen, Wheelwright, Hill, Raste, & Plumb, [2001](#cdev13627-bib-0005)). Many of these aspects of advanced ToM (AToM) are closely linked to children’s social competences, including feelings of loneliness or the ability to form friendships (Bosacki, Moreira, Sitnik, Andrews, & Talwar, [2020](#cdev13627-bib-0010); Caputi, Cugnata, & Brombin, [2020](#cdev13627-bib-0011); Devine, White, Ensor, & Hughes, [2016](#cdev13627-bib-0017); Fink, Begeer, Peterson, Slaughter, & de Rosnay, [2015](#cdev13627-bib-0021); Koerber & Osterhaus, [2020](#cdev13627-bib-0031); Slaughter, Imuta, Peterson, & Henry, [2015](#cdev13627-bib-0051)). Despite its relevance for children’s everyday experiences, there is a paucity of longitudinal work that narrowly tracks the development of AToM in middle childhood across multiple time points and over extensive periods of time.

Early research on children’s AToM focused on their understanding of complex forms of false-belief understanding (Perner & Wimmer, [1985](#cdev13627-bib-0046)). Although children typically develop the explicit understanding that someone may believe something that is not true around the age of 4 years (e.g., Wellman, Cross, & Watson, [2001](#cdev13627-bib-0059)), cross-sectional studies suggest that it is not until 7 or 8 years that children begin to understand more-complex forms of false belief, such as second- or higher order false belief (for a review, see Miller, [2009](#cdev13627-bib-0039)). Second- and higher order false-belief tasks assess whether children understand that someone can hold a false belief about a belief of someone else (e.g., A thinks that B thinks that x…), which is an ability that requires the insight that mental states can be recursive. Understanding the recursive nature of mental states is an important area of children’s cognitive development that is linked to other complex forms of reasoning, such as scientific reasoning (e.g., Astington, Pelletier, & Homer, [2002](#cdev13627-bib-0001); Kyriakopoulou & Vosniadou, [2020](#cdev13627-bib-0033); Osterhaus, Koerber, & Sodian, [2017](#cdev13627-bib-0043)), and it is an aspect of AToM that shows how children may come to acquire more-complex and nuanced theories about other people’s mental lives. In particular, these advanced and nuanced theories of mind may develop when children learn the relevant strategies that allow them to better integrate mental states and to weigh social evidence (Lagattuta et al., [2015](#cdev13627-bib-0034)).

AToM development is, however, not limited to second-order false-belief understanding. Instead, AToM is a broad construct covering diverse social-cognitive skills that develop throughout middle childhood. These include—but are not limited to—the abilities to understand nonliteral speech such as irony or jokes (Happé, [1994](#cdev13627-bib-0024); see also Peterson & Wellman, [2019](#cdev13627-bib-0048); Peterson, Wellman, & Slaughter, [2012](#cdev13627-bib-0049); Wellman, Fang, & Peterson, [2011](#cdev13627-bib-0060)), interpret complex social situations such as recognizing a faux pas (Baron-Cohen et al., [1999](#cdev13627-bib-0004)), interpret emotions and mental states from the eyes (Baron-Cohen et al., [2001](#cdev13627-bib-0005)), and understand that different perspectives can result from the mind’s active interpretation of the external world (Carpendale & Chandler, [1996](#cdev13627-bib-0013)). Cross-sectional work shows that the majority of kindergarten and young elementary school children (i.e., children aged 5–8 years) perform rather poorly on many of these measures of AToM (e.g., Osterhaus & Koerber, [2021](#cdev13627-bib-0041)), with full competencies in many areas not developing until the end of elementary school (e.g., Smogorzewska, Szumski, & Grygiel, [2020](#cdev13627-bib-0052)). There has been some debate as to whether or not the different aspects of AToM cohere (see Warnell & Redcay, [2019](#cdev13627-bib-0057)), but most research reveals significant concurrent as well as longitudinal associations between different tasks when measures are used that are developmentally sensitive and that produce sufficient variation (e.g., Devine et al., [2016](#cdev13627-bib-0017); Hayward & Homer, [2017](#cdev13627-bib-0025); Lecce, Bianco, Devine, & Hughes, [2017](#cdev13627-bib-0035); Meinhardt-Injac, Daum, & Meinhardt, [2020](#cdev13627-bib-0038)).

In this study, we investigate the development of recursive reasoning about mental states in early middle childhood (i.e., from 5 to 8 years) and of yet more-complex and broader aspects of AToM in late middle childhood (i.e., from 9 to 10 years). In early middle childhood, we used two measures (i.e., a higher order false-belief task and a task that involves a double bluff) to assess children’s ability to reason recursively about mental states; in late middle childhood, we used a 15-item battery to investigate the development of broader aspects of AToM (Osterhaus, Koerber, & Sodian, [2016](#cdev13627-bib-0042)). This battery includes the two tasks from early middle childhood and assesses three separate factors, which tap children’s social reasoning (understanding higher order false beliefs and nonliteral speech, and reading off emotions and mental states from the eyes), reasoning about ambiguity (perspective-taking abilities), and recognizing transgressions of social norms (identifying faux pas). Prior work showed that these three factors are largely uncorrelated when general information-processing skills are partialed out (Osterhaus & Koerber, [2021](#cdev13627-bib-0041); Osterhaus et al., [2016](#cdev13627-bib-0042)), suggesting that possibly different mechanisms may operate in the development of AToM. The candidate mechanisms include children’s learning of the strategies that allow to integrate mental states into more-complex theories of mind (Lagattuta et al., [2015](#cdev13627-bib-0034)) and children’s ability to flexibly and appropriately use their understanding of complex mental-state concepts (Devine et al., [2016](#cdev13627-bib-0017)).

Prior longitudinal work has focused on the study of individual differences in AToM. This work has revealed weak associations between preschool ToM and subsequent AToM (Caputi, Lecce, Pagnin, & Banerjee, [2012](#cdev13627-bib-0012); Ebert, [2020](#cdev13627-bib-0018); Guajardo & Cartwright, [2016](#cdev13627-bib-0023); Hughes, [1998](#cdev13627-bib-0027); Lockl & Schneider, [2007](#cdev13627-bib-0037); Peskin, Comay, Chen, & Prusky, [2016](#cdev13627-bib-0047)), as well as a low to moderate stability in AToM itself (Austin, Bondü, & Elsner, [2017](#cdev13627-bib-0002); Banerjee, Watling, & Caputi, [2011](#cdev13627-bib-0003); Devine et al., [2016](#cdev13627-bib-0017); Holl, Kirsch, Rohlf, Krahé, & Elsner, [2018](#cdev13627-bib-0026); Lecce et al., [2017](#cdev13627-bib-0035); Overgaauw, van Duijvenvoorde, Gunther Moor, & Crone, [2015](#cdev13627-bib-0044)). For instance, Devine et al. ([2016](#cdev13627-bib-0017)) investigated middle-childhood (A)ToM development across two measurement points at 6 and 10 years. (A)ToM at 6 years (i.e., first-order false belief; emotion based on false belief; and second-order false belief) independently predicted AToM at 10 years (i.e., the strange stories; silent films; and triangles tasks). There thus is ample evidence that suggests that individual differences in middle-childhood (A)ToM are moderately stable and meaningful. What is missing are longitudinal studies with multiple measures that are repeated within short intervals (e.g., yearly). Such studies are necessary to reveal at which specific age children acquire advanced mental-state concepts and to investigate whether the speed at which they acquire these concepts is relevant for later developments in AToM.

This article reports on such a comprehensive longitudinal study of AToM with yearly measurements over a 5-year period. In particular, we investigated whether developmental differences in early middle childhood (i.e., the age when children first acquire AToM concepts) predict variation in late middle childhood, and whether this variation is (a) stable across time and (b) associated with children’s general information-processing skills (i.e., language abilities, intelligence, and inhibition). Cross-sectional work has revealed significant associations between AToM and language abilities, as well as inhibition (e.g., Wang, Devine, Wong, & Hughes, [2016](#cdev13627-bib-0056)); longitudinal work has shown that language abilities at 6 years predict AToM at 10 years, and that concurrent (but not cross-lagged) associations exist between AToM and executive functions (Devine et al., [2016](#cdev13627-bib-0017)).

The assessments in our study took place on a yearly basis (from 5 to 10 years), across two cohorts (*N* = 161). To investigate children’s development of a core AToM concept (i.e., their understanding of the recursive nature of mental states), we used a higher order false-belief task (Liddle & Nettle, [2006](#cdev13627-bib-0036)) and the double bluff story from the strange stories (Happé, [1994](#cdev13627-bib-0024)) with children aged 5 to 8 years. From an age of 9 years onward, we applied a full AToM battery with 15 tasks that measured 3 AToM factors (Osterhaus et al., [2016](#cdev13627-bib-0042)). Our assessment of children’s information-processing skills comprised measurements of language abilities and intelligence (both at 6 years) and of inhibition (at 10 years).

Based on the prior research reviewed earlier, we expected that children’s development of a core AToM concept (i.e., their understanding of the recursive nature of mental states) would follow a nonlinear pattern, with a plateau phase being reached in early middle childhood. In addition, we expected that children’s general cognitive abilities would predict the age around when the children attain this insight in early elementary school. For late elementary school, we predicted that distinct (i.e., weakly correlated) abilities would operate in AToM cognition, and that only the reasoning components (social reasoning and reasoning about ambiguity) would reveal developmental progressions between Grades 3 and 4. To address the question if the distinct underlying processes are involved in the diverse components of AToM, we explored the associations between the three AToM factors and early AToM competencies, as well as distinct aspects of children’s general cognitive abilities.

## Method

This 5-year longitudinal study is part of a larger project that investigates the relation between the development of AToM and children’s science competencies (see Osterhaus et al., [2017](#cdev13627-bib-0043)); this article is the first report from the project. Across all six measurement points, the children were assessed yearly for AToM and their science competencies (i.e., scientific reasoning and science knowledge). Information-processing skills (e.g., intelligence, language abilities, and inhibition) were assessed during several, but not all measurement points (see Procedure and Figure [1](#cdev13627-fig-0001)). In this report, we use measures of intelligence and language abilities (both at 6 years) and inhibition (at 10 years). The data were collected between June 2014 and July 2019. The average times between assessment waves were as follows: 6.3 months between Kindergarten 0 and Kindergarten 1; 10.0 months between Kindergarten 1 and Grade 1; 11.4 months between Grades 1 and 2; 11.5 months between Grades 2 and 3; and, 11.2 months between Grades 3 and 4. The sample size was chosen based on an a priori power analysis (Faul, Erdfelder, Buchner, & Lang, [2009](#cdev13627-bib-0019)): to be able to detect moderate correlations (*r* =.25) with a power of β =.80, a sample of *N* ≥ 123 is needed.

All study variables were assessed with age-appropriate measures that varied in (a) difficulty and (b) methodology: From 5 to 8 years, we used (mostly) individual, one-on-one interviews; from 9 to 11 years, we relied on group-based testing procedures using paper-and-pencil instruments and closed-response formats (see Materials and Figure [1](#cdev13627-fig-0001)). The group-based testing procedure was chosen as the preferred method in the higher grades (where reading skills are sufficiently high), because it allowed to assess a large, 15-item AToM battery in a cost-effective manner. Group-based testing procedures are not uncommon in studies of AToM in middle childhood (see Devine & Hughes, [2013](#cdev13627-bib-0015); Lecce et al., [2017](#cdev13627-bib-0035)), and there is evidence of test–retest reliability and validity for these procedures (Devine & Hughes, [2016](#cdev13627-bib-0016)). For the 15-item AToM battery, a correlation of *r* =.72 has been reported between paper-and-pencil and classical interview methodologies (Osterhaus et al., [2016](#cdev13627-bib-0042)).

Seventeen children (10.7%) reported, during the measurement at 6 years, that they speak at least one language other than German at home (in Germany, this question is typically used to assess ethnic background). The most commonly reported languages were Turkish (4), Russian (3), and English (2). All other languages (i.e., Arabic, Chinese, French, Pashto, Portuguese, Romanian, Serbian, or Spanish) were each reported only once (see Table [^1]).

Therefore, the sample size reported for a proceeding measurement point can sometimes be lower as for the subsequent one (as, for instance, is the case in Cohort 2 between Kindergarten 0 and Kindergarten 1). The children came from mostly middle-class families and they were recruited from 12 and 5 kindergartens (Cohorts 1 and 2, respectively), and they were followed into 17 elementary schools. The families lived in southern Germany, either in or in close proximity to a midsized city (Cohort 1) or in a more-rural area (Cohort 2). The two-cohort design with one urban and one more rural sample was chosen for two reasons:

First, we were interested in the potential differences between the two cohorts (i.e., between families living in and around a midsized university city vs. families living in an economically strong, yet more rural region). Although we did not expect to find differences with respect to the development of AToM (and indeed, there were no differences in AToM between the cohorts; see below), we were interested in whether there would be differences with respect to the other study variables not reported here (in particular, science competencies, where teachers in urban and more rural areas may use different teaching methods and materials).

Second, the two-cohort design and the inclusion of a more rural sample offered an important methodological advantage: In Germany, the first change of educational setting occurs around 6 years when the children change from kindergarten to elementary school. In larger cities, there is a high density of elementary schools, resulting in a wider distribution of children across different schools. In rural areas, in turn, most children change from a single kindergarten to a single elementary school. Because we tested the children in their schools, and because we were interested in key teacher variables and information pertaining to the instruction that the children had received, we required the consent and continued support from the schools. In Cohort 1 (the urban sample), there were 12 elementary schools and, accordingly, the relative size of the testing groups decreased from kindergarten to elementary school (with, on average, 5.75 students per school, *SD* = 4.22); in Cohort 2 (the rural sample), there were merely 5 schools (with, on average, 11.60 students per school participating in the study, *SD* = 3.78). This more favorable distribution of students across fewer schools (a) made it easier to ensure support by teachers and principals (which in Cohort 2 was acquired when the children were still in kindergarten) and (b) it allowed to reduce the cost of data acquisition (i.e., by making possible the assessment of larger groups of students within a single session, or by reducing travel time to different schools, etc.).

At the end of kindergarten (i.e., during the first wave in Cohort 1 and the second wave in Cohort 2), there were 149 children who participated in the assessment of AToM. Because the study was part of a larger project with multiple assessments being conducted on separate days, some children (*n* = 12) participated in the assessment of AToM during later, but not the first wave.

Of the 149 children who participated at the end of kindergarten, 113 (75.8%) were tested again in Grade 4, resulting in an attrition of 24.2% over the course of 4 years. Of the 36 children who dropped out, 25 (69.4%) did so after the final year of kindergarten, and many of these children remained in kindergarten for an additional year. However, many children dropped out after elementary school, because a considerate number of middle schools were unwilling to further participate in the project (in Germany, there are three types of middle schools: high-, medium-, and low-track schools, which resulted in a wide distribution of children across different schools). ### Child Measures

#### AToM—Understanding the Recursive Nature of Mental States (5–8 Years)

Two tasks assessed children’s developing understanding of the recursive nature of mental states. In the *third-order false-belief task* (Liddle & Nettle, [2006](#cdev13627-bib-0036)), the children are presented with a story about a boy (Nick) who wants to be on the school soccer team. Nick is a passionate player, but he believes that the coach will not select him for the team. The children learn that the coach does want Nick to be on the team. But, the coach knows that Nick believes that he will not be selected. The test question asks the children about the belief of the coach: does he believe that Nick knows that he wants him to be on the team (0 points), or does he know that Nick does not know that he wants him to be on the team (1 point).

In the *double-bluff task* (strange stories; Happé, [1994](#cdev13627-bib-0024)), the children are presented with a story about a soldier who has been taken hostage by his enemies. The enemies ask the soldier where his weapons are hidden. The soldier tells them the true location of the arsenal, and the children are asked why he did that: did he do it because it is the true location (0 points), or because he believes that his enemies would not believe him and hence look for the weapons everywhere but at the true location (1 point)?

Both tasks were presented with support from illustrations, and the children were presented verbally with the two answer options. Answer options were presented to make sure that this interview version was comparable to the paper-and-pencil task version used in the higher grades (see below). Because both tasks imply a simple choice between two options (i.e., the true or false belief of the soccer coach; the true or false location of the arsenal and the motive of the soldier’s claim), it is unlikely that this procedure led to systematic bias in the assessment.

#### AToM—15-Item Battery (9 and 10 Years)

We used a 15-item battery (Osterhaus et al., [2016](#cdev13627-bib-0042)) to assess more-diverse aspects of AToM. This battery measures children’s abilities in social reasoning (including a paper-and-pencil version of the two tasks presented earlier, assessing children’s understanding of the recursive nature of mental states), as well as their ability to recognize transgressions of social norms and to reason about ambiguity. Children’s social reasoning was assessed with tasks about second-order false belief (Astington et al., [2002](#cdev13627-bib-0001)), third- and fourth-order false beliefs (Liddle & Nettle, [2006](#cdev13627-bib-0036)), two strange stories (double bluff and persuasion; Happé, [1994](#cdev13627-bib-0024)), and four items from the Eyes Test (serious, interested, uneasy, and assertive; Baron-Cohen et al., [2001](#cdev13627-bib-0005)). Children’s ability to recognize transgressions of social norms was assessed with three items from the faux-pas task (Baron-Cohen et al., [1999](#cdev13627-bib-0004)) that involved recognizing a faux pas. Children’s ability to reason about ambiguity was measured by assessing their understanding of different interpretations of ambiguous figures. Different story protagonists offered opposing interpretations, and it was the children’s task to choose the best explanation for why they differed in their interpretations (Carpendale & Chandler, [1996](#cdev13627-bib-0013)). Osterhaus et al. ([2016](#cdev13627-bib-0042)) provide more information about all measures included in the AToM battery and they report on a validation study that finds high convergence between the paper-and-pencil and classical interview methodologies.

Although some of the measures that are included in the 15-item AToM battery can be expected to be of low difficulty for children aged 9 and above, a latent class analysis showed that the items included in the battery can successfully distinguish between children with systematic incorrect and correct performance, which most children do not attain before the end of elementary school (10 years) in social reasoning and reasoning about ambiguity (see Osterhaus et al., [2016](#cdev13627-bib-0042)).

#### Inhibition—At 10 Years

We assessed children’s inhibitory control using an adapted paper-and-pencil version of the Stroop test (Stroop, [1935](#cdev13627-bib-0053)). The children had to tick a colored box corresponding to the ink color of a specific color word (blue, gray, green, red, or black). All 36 items of the inferential test had to be answered within 30 s. On average, the children ticked the correct colored box (i.e., the one corresponding to the ink color of the color word, inhibiting what the word reads) on 13.17 items (*SD* = 5.17) within the 30 s. The measure had good reliability, with Cronbach’s α =.89, and there is evidence for the validity of group-based testing procedures for inhibition in elementary school (Obradović, Sulik, Finch, & Tirado-Strayer, [2018](#cdev13627-bib-0040)). We used the measure of inhibition at 10 years (rather than at 8 or 9 years) because the reliability at 8 and 9 years was slightly lower (Cronbach’s α =.87 and.82, respectively). The correlations between the inhibition scores at 8 and 9 years and 9 and 10 years were *r* =.35 (*p* <.001) and *r* =.38 (*p* <.001). The correlation between 8 and 10 years was *r* =.38 (*p* <.001).

#### Intelligence—At 6 Years

We used the 10-item “analogical reasoning” subtest of the Vienna Development Test (Kastner-Koller & Deimann, [2002](#cdev13627-bib-0029)) as a proxy for children’s intelligence. The children have to choose 1 of 5 elements that completes a 3 × 3 matrix (color, form, and size) in a logical sequence. On average, the children obtained a correct score of 7.65 points (*SD* = 2.57). The test reliability was good, with Cronbach’s α =.83.

#### Language—At 6 Years

We measured children’s understanding of spoken language using the subtest “language receptive” of the Intelligence and Developmental Scales (Grob, Meyer, & Hagmann-von Arx, [2009](#cdev13627-bib-0022)). The children used small toy figures to replay 12 sentences of increasing grammatical complexity (e.g., “the girl who is being chased by the boy is holding the cloth that before was in the cat basket”). Full credit (1 point) was given for replaying the content of the sentence in the correct order, and partial credit (0.5 points) was given for correct content in the incorrect order. On average, the children obtained a score of 4.11 points (*SD* = 1.93). This measure showed a good reliability, with Cronbach’s α =.88.

### Procedure

The individual interviews were conducted on five separate days by trained researchers in quiet, separate rooms at the children’s kindergartens or schools. The (kindergarten) teachers were not present during the interviews. At 5 and 6 years, the two AToM measures (third-order false belief and double bluff) were assessed together with an assessment of children’s understanding of the nature of science (Koerber & Osterhaus, [2019](#cdev13627-bib-0030); Interview 1, see Figure [1](#cdev13627-fig-0001)). At 7 and 8 years, the same two AToM tasks were administered following the assessments of language abilities and intelligence (Interview 4, see Figure [1](#cdev13627-fig-0001)). Interview 4 was, across all measurement points, conducted on the fourth day of testing; the order of Interviews 1–3 was randomized, and consequently, some children were assessed for AToM on the first day of the project, whereas others were assessed on the second or third day. The sequence of the tasks within a single interview did, however, not vary: AToM was always proceeded by an assessment of the nature of science (at 5 and 6 years) or the assessment of intelligence (at 7 and 8 years). No (corrective) feedback was given to any answer of the children.

From 9 years onward, AToM was assessed with a 15-item, paper-and-pencil battery that made possible the use of a group-based testing procedure. All 15 items were presented during a testing session (see Figure [1](#cdev13627-fig-0001): Paper-and-pencil session 1) that took approximately 60 min. The assessment of AToM was proceeded by assessments of scientific reasoning, science knowledge, and intelligence. All children were given an individual booklet with illustrated task versions. The task content and the answer options were read out by trained research assistants and, in addition, all tasks were presented in a PowerPoint® presentation (the presentation can be obtained from the authors). One to two test assistants were present during the administration of the tasks to make sure that children work quietly on their own booklets and do not copy from each other. The teachers were often, but not always present during the assessments. Privacy shields were used so that the teachers, researchers, and children’s peers could not read their answers.

### Understanding the Recursive Nature of Mental States: Development, Age at Acquisition, and Predictors

We assessed whether the development of children’s understanding of the recursive nature of mental states (i.e., the “mastery” score for the third-order false-belief and double-bluff tasks) follows a linear pattern by assessing the goodness of fit of several (non)linear models (Bornkamp, Pinheiro, & Bretz, [2019](#cdev13627-bib-0006)): the linear model, the exponential, the logistic, and the quadratic model (see Figure [4](#cdev13627-fig-0004)). We used the Akaike information criterion (AIC) to select the best-fitting, most parsimonious model. The quadratic model (AIC = 106.09) fitted the data substantially better than did the exponential (AIC = 121.11), linear (AIC = 109.29), and logistic (AIC = 113.11) models. The good fit of the quadratic model reflects the large increase in performance until 7 years of age and the subsequent plateau phase (see Figures [2](#cdev13627-fig-0002) and [3](#cdev13627-fig-0003)) and it supports the idea that there is a further conceptual insight involved in middle-childhood AToM.

The mean age when children first acquired an understanding of the recursive nature of mental states (i.e., when they first answered both the third-order false-belief and double-bluff tasks correctly) was 7 years and 1.2 months (*SD* = 12.45 months). In second grade (at 8 years), there were only four children (3%) who gave wrong answers to both the double-bluff and third-order false-belief tasks.

In a hierarchical regression analysis, we tested whether children’s intelligence at 6 years (Model 1) and their intelligence and language abilities at 6 years (Model 2) predicted the age at acquisition. Model 1 was significant \[*F* (1, 117) = 4.56, *p* =.035\], indicating that higher intelligence scores were associated with being younger when acquiring an understanding of the recursive nature of mental states (β = –.19, *p* =.035). Adding language abilities at 6 years as an additional predictor to the model did not result in a higher amount of explained variance \[Model 1: *R* <sup>2</sup> =.038; Model 2: *R* <sup>2</sup> =.053; change in *F* = 1.89, change in *p* =.172\], suggesting that children’s abilities in higher order belief reasoning cannot simply be attributed to the high information-processing demands of AToM tasks, as suggested by the complexity-only hypothesis (Sullivan, Zaitchik, & Tager-Flusberg, [1994](#cdev13627-bib-0054); remember that our test of language abilities assessed the children for their understanding of complex syntax by presenting them with short, but complex stories that they had to act out using little toy figures).

### Stability in AToM and Performance Differences Across Measures

Figure [4](#cdev13627-fig-0004) shows the cross-lagged relations between the children’s performance in AToM over time. There was moderate stability in children’s understanding of the recursive nature of mental states at 6 and 7 years (correlations between.24 and.33), which is the age around which the children acquired an understanding of these concepts (see above; see also e.g., Perner & Wimmer, [1985](#cdev13627-bib-0046); Sullivan et al., [1994](#cdev13627-bib-0054)). There was a significant concurrent correlation between the third-order false-belief and the double-bluff tasks at 7 years, and there was a significant cross-lagged correlation between double bluff (at 6 years) and third-order false belief (at 7 years).

![](https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/cms/asset/2a3eaac6-3d67-4522-b9c9-95976510ef70/cdev13627-fig-0004-m.jpg)

Figure 4 Open in figure viewer PowerPoint Spearman correlations (left; from Kindergarten 0 to Grade 2) and Pearson correlations (right; in Grades 3 and 4) between advanced theory-of-mind measures.

There was low stability in AToM in Grades 3 and 4, with no significant intergrade correlation for social reasoning (*r* =.14, *p* =.137) or recognizing transgressions of social norms (*r* =.16, *p* =.095), with the correlation only being significant for reasoning about ambiguity (*r* =.24, *p* =.011; see Figure [4](#cdev13627-fig-0004)). This last correlation remained significant when controlling for language abilities, intelligence, and inhibition (ρ =.24, *p* =.011). In addition, there was a cross-lagged correlation between social reasoning (at 9 years) and reasoning about ambiguity (at 10 years).

## Discussion

How AToM develops throughout middle childhood (in children aged 5–10 years) and whether children’s acquisition of core AToM concepts in kindergarten and early elementary school predicts performance in more-diverse aspects of late elementary-school AToM are the two main questions of this longitudinal study.

Our main finding is that a core concept in children’s AToM develops nonlinearly, and involves a milestone being reached at the age of 7 years, around when children attain the conceptual insight that mental states can be recursive. Understanding the recursive nature of mental states is considered to be one of the core conceptual insights that children need to acquire before they can solve AToM tasks, and in particular measures of second-order false belief (the conceptual-development hypothesis; Perner, [1988](#cdev13627-bib-0045)). Finding a nonlinear (rather than gradual) development in children’s ability to reason about recursive mental states provides evidence in favor of the hypothesis that it is indeed a conceptual insight that is involved in the development of this core aspect of AToM.

That a conceptual development is involved in AToM is in line with our finding that information-processing skills did not explain the variation in the children’s age at acquisition of the understanding of the recursive nature of mental states. It has been suggested that AToM and measures of second-order false belief are more difficult than measures of first-order ToM simply because solving these tasks requires children to keep track of and to reason about complex events (the complexity-only hypothesis; Sullivan et al., [1994](#cdev13627-bib-0054)). Indeed, children tend to solve a second-order false-belief task correctly at a younger age when the linguistic complexity of the task is reduced (Sullivan et al., [1994](#cdev13627-bib-0054)), suggesting that superficial task characteristics may increase or reduce task difficulty. Finding no association between children’s receptive language abilities (which we measured with a test that required the children to keep track of complex syntax and events) and their age at acquisition provides evidence against the hypothesis that it is complexity alone that makes AToM advanced.

Whereas the children’s developing conceptual understanding of the recursive nature of mental states in early middle childhood (i.e., between the ages of 5–8 years) followed a nonlinear pattern, with a plateau phase after the age of 7 years, there were still developmental progressions in social reasoning and reasoning about ambiguity in late middle childhood (i.e., from 9 years onwards), with significant differences emerging between Grades 3 and 4. This result shows that AToM continues to develop through the entire course of elementary school, possibly by means of an integration of more-complex mental states (Lagattuta et al., [2015](#cdev13627-bib-0034)).

The integration of more-complex mental states in children’s advanced theories of mind may be a key mechanism that drives the development of AToM (Lagattuta et al., [2015](#cdev13627-bib-0034)). Within this developmental process, second-order false belief may well be one of the first complex mental states that is integrated in children’s AToM. Once children attain the insight that mental states can be recursive, a new level of complexity and an understanding of the richness of mental states may open up, possibly marking the beginning of an integration of yet more-complex mental states. The finding that higher order false-belief reasoning and children’s ability to read off complex mental states from the eyes (Eyes Test; Baron-Cohen et al., [2001](#cdev13627-bib-0005)) load on a single factor (Osterhaus et al., [2016](#cdev13627-bib-0042)), as well as our finding here that significant associations exist between complex AToM reasoning and intelligence are in line with this interpretation. In particular, it seems reasonable to assume that the children with higher scores on our measure of intelligence find it easier to interpret and weigh rich “social evidence,” integrating this evidence and more-complex mental states into their AToM (Lagattuta et al., [2015](#cdev13627-bib-0034)). The integration of more-complex mental states may then be indicative of conceptual advances that include, for instance, the understanding that mental processes affect knowledge construction or that people may differ in their interpretation of the very same situation.

The integration of more-complex mental states may, however, not be involved in all AToM development. It is worth noting that we found a distinct pattern of results for children’s ability to recognize transgressions of social norms (i.e., the faux-pas task; Baron-Cohen et al., [1999](#cdev13627-bib-0004)): Whereas the AToM factors social reasoning and reasoning about ambiguity were associated with general information-processing skills, neither intelligence nor language abilities predicted recognizing transgressions of social norms in Grades 3 or 4. Instead, children’s ability to recognize a faux pas was predicted by the speed at which the children acquired the core AToM concept of understanding the recursive nature of mental states in early middle childhood (i.e., the children’s age at acquisition).

Cross-sectional studies found weak associations between the three AToM factors, and Osterhaus et al. ([2016](#cdev13627-bib-0042)) discuss whether the recognition of a transgression of a social norm may not require extensive reasoning processes, but rather some kind of “monitoring process.” Devine et al. ([2016](#cdev13627-bib-0017)) suggest that children’s ToM development in middle childhood involves increases in performance, that is, an increased ability to appropriately and flexibly use and apply AToM concepts. Finding a significant correlation between recognizing transgressions of social norms and the age at acquisition of a core AToM concept is in line with this proposed mechanism, which suggests a positive effect of practice: The sooner the children attain foundational AToM concepts, the more frequently they will encounter occasions where they can apply these concepts. Applying AToM concepts in everyday life will likely result in direct feedback in specific social situations, and hence the children will increase their proficiency to flexibly and appropriately use AToM concepts (Devine et al., [2016](#cdev13627-bib-0017)).

When it comes to recognizing faux pas, this process seems to be concluded in mid-elementary school: As opposed to the other two AToM factors, we found no improvement in children’s ability to recognize transgressions of social norms between Grades 3 and 4. This finding is in line with other studies that report significant development in recognizing faux pas between 7.5 and 8.5 years, but not between 8.5 and 9.5 years (Smogorzewska et al., [2020](#cdev13627-bib-0052)). One may speculate and interpret this finding as showing that, by mid-elementary school, the children may have had sufficient opportunity to experience and practice the application of AToM concepts with respect to this particular aspect of AToM (i.e., the ability to recognize a faux pas).

There is, however, an alternative interpretation that holds that the null finding regarding the development in recognizing transgressions of social norms between Grades 3 and 4 is due to a lack of variance in our AToM battery (correct performance was overall high, > 80% correct for several aspects). Finding significant correlations between children’s ability to recognize faux pas in Grade 3 and their age at acquisition (understanding the recursive nature of mental states), as well as between general information-processing skills and the two reasoning aspects of AToM, however, makes this interpretation less plausible and suggests that the variance that was picked up by the AToM battery was overall not too low to detect meaningful associations.

### Limitations and Future Directions

The children’s performance in AToM that we documented at the end of elementary school was not particularly stable, with autocorrelations in Grades 3 and 4 ranging between.14 (nonsignificant) and.24. Researchers typically find somewhat higher (yet overall low to moderate) stability in middle-childhood AToM (Austin et al., [2017](#cdev13627-bib-0002); Banerjee et al., [2011](#cdev13627-bib-0003); Devine et al., [2016](#cdev13627-bib-0017); Holl et al., [2018](#cdev13627-bib-0026); Lecce et al., [2017](#cdev13627-bib-0035); Overgaauw et al., [2015](#cdev13627-bib-0044)). The present study’s failure to detect such associations may have been a consequence of the children’s relatively high performance, especially on the factor recognizing transgressions of social norms, where an average performance > 80% correct was obtained in Grade 3. Future research should—next to the closed-response questions used here—make use of open-ended answer formats, which are less sensitive to guessing and may result in finding larger amounts of variance. The recent development of automated scoring procedures for AToM tasks (Kovatchev et al., [2020](#cdev13627-bib-0032)) presents a great advance that makes possible the use of such open-response formats in a cost-effective manner in larger studies with multiple repeated measures.

The tasks used in this study represent the most frequently used measures in the study of middle-childhood AToM. Nonetheless, several other conceptually and methodologically interesting tasks have been developed to study AToM, including for instance the silent films task (Devine & Hughes, [2013](#cdev13627-bib-0015)), the socially ambiguous stories (Bosacki & Astington, [1999](#cdev13627-bib-0009)), or the triangles tasks (Castelli, Happé, Frith, & Frith, [2000](#cdev13627-bib-0014)). Future work should address if the findings and interpretations reported here can generalize to these and other measures of AToM.

Another shortcoming of this study relates to the relatively high rate of attrition (24%). Most of the drop out occurred between kindergarten and Grade 1, where 70% of the participants who dropped out were lost. Attrition from Grades 1 to 4 was below 10%, and the general results did not differ between kindergarten and elementary school. Therefore, we are confident that attrition did not induce systematic bias in the study.

Attrition in longitudinal studies is often particularly high when measurement points span across periods where children move from one type of school to another (e.g., from kindergarten to elementary school). In Cohort 2 of this study, we drew a sample of children living in a more rural area, allowing us to better predict the educational paths of the children (in rural areas, there are fewer schools that families can choose from, and so most children change from a single kindergarten to a single school). Accordingly, Cohort 2 had substantially larger groups of children participating in each school. Having large groups of participants within a single school reduces the barriers to participation, and meta-analyses suggest that barrier-reduction strategies have a strong positive effect on retention rates (Teague et al., [2018](#cdev13627-bib-0055)). Another strategy that has been suggested to reduce attrition is the use of online testing procedures (see e.g., Sheskin et al., [2020](#cdev13627-bib-0050)). On-line testing procedures may also reduce response biases due to social desirability, and in longitudinal studies that are conducted within educational settings, it can help to avoid the drop-out of participants who move away from the area where the study is conducted.

Our sample was mostly German, with around 10% of the children reporting a diverse ethnic background. This percentage is comparable to the German average, which, in 2014, was reported to be 11.3% (Federal Agency of Migration & Refugees, [2014](#cdev13627-bib-0020)). There were more children with a diverse ethnic background in Cohort 1 (the urban sample from a university city) than in Cohort 2 (the rural sample), and parents in Cohort 1 were, overall, higher educated than parents in Cohort 2. Despite these differences, we found no differences in AToM development between the two cohorts, suggesting that our findings generalize across populations. Nonetheless, future research must address the question if AToM development follows a similar path across diverse populations, especially for children who are not from Western, educated, industrialized, rich, and democratic (WEIRD) societies.

### Conclusion

This longitudinal study has documented significant developmental progress in diverse aspects of AToM cognition in children aged 5–10 years. Our findings add to the growing body of research showing that ToM development is not confined to preschool, with a second milestone occurring around the age of 7 years. Our findings suggest that different mechanisms may account for the development of diverse aspects of AToM, which is a finding that should guide future research on the consequences of AToM. Researchers have identified diverse consequences of AToM, including children’s everyday social experiences (e.g., Fink et al., [2015](#cdev13627-bib-0021)), their self-understanding (e.g., Bosacki, [2000](#cdev13627-bib-0007), [2015](#cdev13627-bib-0008); Bosacki et al., [2020](#cdev13627-bib-0010)), as well as their epistemological beliefs (e.g., Weinstock, Israel, Cohen, Tabak, & Harari, [2020](#cdev13627-bib-0058)) or their academic abilities (e.g., Ebert, [2020](#cdev13627-bib-0018); Lecce et al., [2017](#cdev13627-bib-0035)). Because these are variables that are of fundamental importance to children’s success in life, as well as their psychological well-being, understanding the development of AToM in middle childhood is not only a theoretically interesting question, but also a practically relevant one that needs to be answered in order to better understand this important aspect of children’s developing social cognition.
