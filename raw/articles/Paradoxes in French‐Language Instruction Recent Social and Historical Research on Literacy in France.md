A B S T R A C T  
This essay on three recent books by outstanding scholars— La raison scolaire by Bernard Lahire,Histoire de l’enseignement  
du français du XVII e au XX e siècle by André Chervel, and L’école et la lecture obligatoire by Anne-Marie Chartier — offers  
the opportunity to acquaint readers with French research on teaching written language and to show the value of historical  
research for understanding past and current developments. The discussion highlights issues of inequality and failure in the  
face of the arbitrary and changing nature of schooled literacy and the vital role that teachers have played in redefining the  
everyday meaning and craft of teaching.

outstanding books on literacy and schooling  
in France, with two purposes in mind. First,  
we hope to foster a productive dialogue between two  
strong but at times distant fields of research, one set in  
the Anglo-American world, and the other steeped in the  
French scholarly tradition. Social and historical research  
on literacy in France has reached English-language  
readers through the translated works of François Furet

Michel de Certeau (1980), and Roger Chartier (1994,  
2007), yet much research remains virtually unknown.  
Scholars in France have had access to translations of  
Richard Hoggart, Robert Darnton, Elizabeth Eisen-  
stein, Jack Goody, and David Olson and have recently  
addressed the challenges posed by New Literacy Stud-  
ies (e.g., Fraenkel & Mbodj, 2010).  
Both currents of thought have influenced studies on  
reading and writing in school, yet mutual knowledge of  
this research has been strangely limited by institutional  
and academic constraints. In fact, an important group  
of French sociologists, anthropologists, and historians  
has contributed studies that invite deep reflection on  
the situated and historical nature of literacy and lit-  
eracies, although the term literacy, translated with the  
neologism litteracie, is still the object of controversy in  
France, where culture écrite (written culture) is often  
preferred. The influence of this scholarly tradition on  
European and Latin American research suggests that  
English-language scholars might do well to explore this  
growing international community. Our second pur-  
pose is to show that historical research on the relation of  
reading and writing to schooling is of vital importance  
for comprehending the current paradoxes of literacy in  
contemporary school settings.  
These three recent publications represent the life-  
long work of three distinguished French scholars.  
We begin with sociologist Bernard Lahire’s book  
on the logic of schooling,La Raison Scolaire (The  
Logic of Schooling), which both develops and takes  
issue with the classic explanation of school failure  
widely disseminated through Pierre Bourdieu and  
Jean-Claude Passeron’s (1970) book La Reproduction.  
Lahire develops the concept of a scriptal-schooled  
relation to the French language to stress the role of  
formal schooling in producing the inequities among  
students of different classes.  
We then reconsider Lahire’s representation of  
school failure by introducing the detailed versions  
of the history of French schooling offered by André  
Chervel and Anne-Marie Chartier. Chervel’s Histoire  
de l’Enseignement du Français du XVII e au XX e Siècle  
(The History of the Teaching of French From the 17th to  
the 20th Centuries) examines the slow but deliberate  
construction of the French language as a school subject,  
with its evolving relations among orthography, gram-  
mar, literature, and composition, and proposes that this  
particular cultural process has had an enduring effect  
on French culture generally. Anne-Marie Chartier, in  
L’École et la Lecture Obligatoire (Schooling and Compul-  
sory Reading), draws on her historical research on read-  
ing since the 17th century. Adopting the perspective of  
teachers in their day-to-day practice, Chartier takes the  
reader through the long history of the meanings of liter-  
acy and their relation to changing cultural and political  
contexts. She focuses on the numerous contradictions  
and paradoxes, such as the production of failure, that  
have emerged as reading, and later schooling, were  
made compulsory.  
The three volumes together offer distinct and  
complementary views of the relationship of written  
language and schooling. The long-term historical per-  
spective adds temporal depth to the abstract distinc-  
tions between written and oral language, and between  
schooled and everyday uses of literacy, by setting them  
within the changing cultural configurations of French  
culture and society. Our analysis suggests that these  
perspectives challenge many taken-for-granted assump-  
tions on the nature of schooled literacy discussed in  
other scholarly traditions.  
Lahire: The Acquisition  
of a Scriptal-Schooled  
Relation to Language  
Lahire is currently a professor of sociology at the École  
Normale Supérieure, Lettres et Sciences Humaines,  
in Lyon, France, where he heads a research group  
on socialization. Over the past two decades, he has  
published over a dozen books (several translated into  
other languages), has been invited to lecture at the  
principal universities in Europe and Latin America,  
and has spent time as a visiting scholar at the University  
of California, Berkeley. In La Raison Scolaire, Lahire  
offers an excellent synthesis of his previous studies on  
social inequality and written culture (1993, 1995) and  
elaborates on the theoretical scheme which sustains  
his sociological project by addressing the opposition  
between la raison pratique (practical reason) and la  
raison scolaire (school reason, or the logic of schooling).  
He supports his perspective through detailed analyses  
of classroom interaction and textual production in the  
elementary grades, as well as reading and writing prac-  
tices of families and college students.  
In the preface, Lahire accepts Bourdieu’s basic the-  
sis of a class-based inequality in the accumulation cul-  
tural capital made possible largely through early access  
to the written code at home (p. 33). However, he takes  
issue with Bourdieu for not recognizing the powerful  
influence of schooling in the construction of an abstract  
reflexive relation to language, beyond the specific cul-  
tural capital that children inherit through their primary  
socialization (pp. 16–17, 48). Lahire situates his argu-  
ment within the context of modern French school-  
ing—and is careful not to generalize—recounting its  
history from prerevolutionary times through the reforms  
implanted by Jules Ferry during the last quarter of the  
19th century and stressing the specific and privileged  
status of French-language instruction. He outlines the
