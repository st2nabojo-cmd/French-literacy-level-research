## Summary

Jeanne Chall's 1983 model describes six stages of reading development from birth to adulthood. The first three stages (Stages 0–2, roughly ages 0–8) map directly to Nabojo's Tier 1 and Tier 2 readers. Stage 1 (ages 6–7, Tier 1) is characterised by the cognitive effort of decoding — all working memory goes to translating letters to sounds. Stage 2 (ages 7–8.6, Tier 2) is a fluency-building stage where familiar content allows the reader to practise decoding without the additional burden of processing new ideas. Stage 3 is the first "reading to learn" stage, corresponding to Tiers 3–4.

## Key Findings / Arguments

1. **Stage 0 (birth–6):** Pre-reading; children accumulate knowledge of letters, words, rhyme, alliteration. Not directly relevant to Nabojo's target range but grounds understanding of Tier 1 entry state.
2. **Stage 1 (ages 6–7 = Tier 1):** "The essential aspect... is learning the arbitrary set of letters and associating these with the corresponding parts of spoken words." The qualitative change at the end of Stage 1 is insight into the spelling system. All cognitive resources are consumed by decoding — comprehension is secondary.
3. **Stage 2 (ages 7–8.6 = Tier 2):** "Reading in Stage 2 consolidates what was learned in Stage 1." **Crucially: Stage 2 reading is NOT for gaining new information but for confirming what is already known.** Because content is familiar, readers can direct attention to the printed words. Fluency and speed develop here.
4. **Stage 3 (= Tiers 3–4 onset):** Reading begins to serve learning. Materials must be "clear, within one viewpoint, and limited in technical complexities." Still not ready for multiple viewpoints.
5. **Stage 4 (ages 14–18):** Multiple viewpoints become accessible. Beyond Nabojo's range.

## Relevant Data

- Stage 1 ages: **6–7** (Grades 1–2)
- Stage 2 ages: **7–8.6** (Grades 2–3)
- Stage 3 onset: **~age 9** (Grades 4+)
- Stage 2 key constraint: **content must be familiar** — not informational novelty

## Tier Implications

### Tier 1 (Manner): Stage 1 Constraints
Tier 1 readers are in Chall's Stage 1: every cognitive resource goes to decoding. This has direct Manner implications:
- Sentences must be as short as possible — any added complexity competes with the decoding effort
- Vocabulary must be extremely familiar — new words must appear in richly supportive context
- Stories cannot demand inferential comprehension — what happens must be stated, not implied
- Repetition of sentence patterns is not stylistically weak; it is cognitively necessary

### Tier 2 (Matter + Manner): Stage 2 Constraints
The most surprising implication of Chall for Nabojo: **Tier 2 stories should use familiar topics.** Stage 2 readers build fluency by reading about things they already know. Introducing entirely new conceptual territory (unfamiliar settings, exotic characters, novel knowledge) adds cognitive load that undermines the fluency goal.
- **Matter:** Prefer familiar settings (school, home, neighbourhood) even for Tier 2. Adventures can have novelty in plot but not in world.
- **Manner:** High-frequency vocabulary is the target, not vocabulary enrichment. New words should be rare and contextually supported.

### Tier 3 (Matter): First Reading-to-Learn Stage
Tier 3 is the transition into Chall's Stage 3. Stories can now introduce new knowledge, new settings, new concepts — provided they present a single, clear viewpoint. No moral ambiguity, no unreliable narrators.

### Tier 4 (Matter): Approaching Stage 4
Tier 4 (ages 10–12) sits at the upper bound of Chall's Stage 3 and the lower edge of Stage 4. Multiple viewpoints are emerging but not yet fully accessible. Moral complexity should be introduced carefully — always with resolution or at minimum authorial framing.

## Limitations / Bias

- Article is a web summary/excerpt of Chall (1983); the original book contains more nuance.
- Stages were developed based on English-language reading; French orthography is more opaque (harder to decode), which may push Stage 1 demands higher for French readers — potentially extending Stage 1 constraints into early Tier 2.
- Age ranges are approximations; individual variation is significant.

## Raw Quotes

> "Stage 1: The essential aspect of Stage 1 is learning the arbitrary set of letters and associating these with the corresponding parts of spoken words."

> "Stage 2: Essentially, reading in Stage 2 consolidates what was learned in Stage 1. Reading stories previously heard increases fluency. **Stage 2 reading is not for gaining new information, but for confirming what is already known to the reader.**"

> "Stage 3: Because their background (world) knowledge, vocabulary, and cognitive abilities are still limited at this stage, the first steps of Stage 3 reading are usually best developed with materials and purposes that are clear, within one viewpoint, and limited in technical complexities."
