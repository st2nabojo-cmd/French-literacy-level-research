## Summary

A publishing industry guide distinguishing three book formats for young readers using the *Fancy Nancy* series as a worked example: picture books (read TO a child), early readers (read BY a child), and chapter books (for immersed readers). The distinctions are operationally precise and directly applicable to Nabojo's Manner constraints. Early readers map to Tier 1 (ages 6–7) and transitional chapter books map to Tier 2 (ages 7–8). The article provides specific word counts, sentence structure rules, and cognitive load constraints for each format.

## Key Findings / Arguments

1. **Picture books (not directly relevant):** ~420 words; designed to be read *to* a child; illustrations go beyond the text; no strict word-choice rules.
2. **Early readers (~567 words):** Designed to be read *by* a child. Short sentences. Simple words at grade level. Big, easy-to-read typeface. Lots of white space. **Sentences are completed on one page — no sentence runs from one page to the next.** Illustrations support (but don't exceed) the text.
3. **Early reader cognitive constraint:** "Readers at this level are working very hard to decode words, they aren't able to keep track of more than a few characters at a time or to follow complex stories. Deciphering a whole sentence is a huge accomplishment at this stage. Keeping multiple story elements in mind at the same time is simply too hard."
4. **Chapter books (~8,700 words):** Smaller typeface, less white space, complex sentences mixed with simple, varied vocabulary, words hyphenated at line ends, sentences run across pages, multiple named characters, multiple simultaneous plot elements.
5. **Transitional/first chapter books:** Publishers (Scholastic Acorn, Aladdin Quix, etc.) have created in-between formats for readers who have graduated from levelled reading programs but aren't ready for 7,500+ word chapter books. This corresponds to Tier 2.
6. **Character count constraint:** At early reader level, only 2–3 characters should be named. At chapter book level, the reader tracks a whole cast.

## Relevant Data

- **Picture book:** ~420 words
- **Early reader (I Can Read Level 1):** ~567 words + glossary
- **First/transitional chapter book:** typically 2,000–5,000 words (implied by publisher programs)
- **Full chapter book:** ~7,500–8,700 words
- **Early reader sentence rule:** sentence completed on a single page, never runs over
- **Early reader format:** big typeface, lots of white space, illustrations on every page

## Tier Implications

### Tier 1 (Manner): Early Reader Format
Nabojo's Tier 1 content should be conceived in early reader format constraints:
- Total text per session: equivalent scale to an early reader (300–600 words of text accessible at one sitting)
- Sentence length: short enough to fit comfortably on one screen without line-wrap confusion
- Characters: 2–3 named maximum; protagonist + 1–2 supporting
- Typeface and white space: the e-ink display should use generous spacing and large text
- Illustrations on every screen — text supported (not exceeded) by images

### Tier 2 (Manner): Transitional Chapter Book Format
Tier 2 maps to the transitional/first chapter book zone:
- Word count per chapter/episode: 500–2,000 words
- Begin introducing sentences that are more complex (can span multiple clauses)
- More named characters — but still limited (4–6 maximum)
- Less illustration density — text carries more of the load
- Vocabulary slightly more varied; occasional unfamiliar word contextually supported

## Raw Quotes

> "Readers at this level are working very hard to decode words, they aren't able to keep track of more than a few characters at a time or to follow complex stories.
