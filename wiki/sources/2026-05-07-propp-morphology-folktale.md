## Summary

Vladimir Propp's 1928 foundational work on fairy tale structure, available in English translation (2nd ed., 1968). Propp analysed 100 Russian fairy tales and identified 31 recurring *functions* — abstract narrative actions like "Villain causes harm," "Hero leaves home," "Hero is tested by donor," "Villain is defeated," "Hero is recognised" — that appear in a consistent sequence. Characters are interchangeable (the villain can be a dragon or a witch or a stepmother), but the functions they perform are constant. This is the structural complement to the Hero's Journey: Propp provides a granular sequential inventory of narrative events, while the Hero's Journey provides the three-act arc with character archetypes. Together they form the foundation of [[narrative-morphology]].

## Key Findings / Arguments

1. **31 narrative functions, sequentially ordered.** Not all tales use all 31 functions, but those used always appear in the same sequence. A tale might use functions 1, 3, 8, 11, 14, 20, 25, 31 — but never in a different order.
2. **Key function clusters relevant to Nabojo:**
   - *Absentation* (function 1): a family member leaves home — the triggering absence
   - *Interdiction* (function 2): a rule is given to the hero ("don't go into the forest")
   - *Violation* (function 3): the rule is violated — tension begins
   - *Villainy/Lack* (function 8): the villain causes harm, or the hero lacks something
   - *Departure* (function 11): the hero leaves on a quest
   - *Donor test* (function 12): the hero is tested by a donor figure
   - *Hero acquires magical agent* (function 14): tool or helper received
   - *Villain defeated* (function 18): the climax
   - *Return* (function 20): the hero comes back
   - *Recognition* (function 27): the hero is recognised as hero
   - *Wedding/Throne* (function 31): resolution and reward
3. **Characters are defined by their function (sphere of action), not their surface traits.** Propp identifies seven spheres of action: Villain, Donor, Helper, Princess+Father, Dispatcher, Hero, False Hero. These map loosely but not directly to the Hero's Journey archetypes.
4. **Structural analysis is syntagmatic (linear/sequential).** This is distinct from Lévi-Strauss's paradigmatic (oppositional) analysis. Propp lays out the *order* of events; Lévi-Strauss looks for underlying binary oppositions.
5. **Cross-cultural applicability is debated.** Propp's analysis is definitively of Russian fairy tales (Afanasiev collection, tales 50–151). Subsequent scholars have found partial applicability to African, American Indian, and other traditions. French *contes de fées* (Perrault, Aulnoy, Leprince de Beaumont) share many but not all Proppian functions.

## Tier Implications

### Tier 1 (Matter): Functions 1, 8, 11, 18, 20, 31 — Minimal Arc
A Tier 1 story can be built from the smallest functional core:
- Absentation (or Lack): something is missing or someone is gone
- Villainy/Harm: a problem arises
- Departure: the hero sets out
- Villain defeated (or problem solved): concrete, visual resolution
- Return: back home, reward

### Tier 2 (Matter): Adding Donor and Helper Functions
- Introduce the Donor test (function 12): the hero proves worthiness before receiving help
- Introduce the Helper (function 14): a magical agent, friend, or tool joins the hero
- The False Hero (function 24+) is too complex for Tier 2 — avoid

### Tier 3 (Matter): Extended Function Sequence
- The full Act 2 sequence of tests, helpers, and approach to the climax is manageable
- Subplots involving a Dispatcher or multiple helpers add welcome complexity

### Tier 4 (Matter): Subversion and Variation
- The False Hero becomes viable — a character who claims to be the hero but isn't
- Functions can be distributed across multiple simultaneous arcs
- Propp's fixed sequence can be consciously subverted for narrative effect

## Limitations / Bias

- Only the introduction and table of contents were extracted from this 185-page book; the specific function definitions (Chapter III) were not read. This source page will need updating when Chapter III is extracted.
- The Russian fairy tale corpus is culturally specific. French conte de fées tradition differs.
- Propp's model is *descriptive* of a corpus, not prescriptive for all story types. Interactive branching narratives (choose-your-own-adventure) may require adaptation — functions at choice points can branch, meaning the sequence is no longer linear.
