## Summary

An essay reviewing three French scholarly works on the history of literacy and schooling in France: Lahire's *La Raison Scolaire* (sociological analysis of school failure through the lens of "scriptal-schooled relation to language"), Chervel's *Histoire de l'Enseignement du Français* (history of French as a school subject from 17th–20th century), and Chartier's *L'École et la Lecture Obligatoire* (history of reading instruction and its paradoxes). The article is primarily historical and sociological, not pedagogical. Its direct pipeline value is limited, but it provides important cultural context: French literacy instruction has a specific, institutionalised character that makes "schooled French" qualitatively different from everyday French, with class-based access implications.

## Key Findings / Arguments

1. **"Scriptal-schooled relation to language" (Lahire).** French schooling constructs a formal, abstract, reflexive relation to written language that goes beyond cultural capital inherited at home. Children from non-literary households face a double burden: they must acquire both the language skills and the specific, schooled form of those skills.
2. **"Literacy" is contested in France.** The Anglo-American concept of literacy (*litteracie*) is a neologism in France; the preferred term is *culture écrite* (written culture). This reflects a different conception of what reading and writing mean culturally.
3. **French as a school subject has a unique, privileged status.** Chervel documents the slow construction of French as a school subject with its own internal logic (orthography, grammar, literature, composition) that has had an enduring effect on French culture.
4. **Inequality is structural.** School failure is not random; it is systematically produced by the mismatch between the form of schooled literacy and the cultural practices of working-class families.
5. **Teachers redefine literacy in practice.** Chartier shows that teachers throughout history have adapted formal reading instruction to classroom realities — creating productive paradoxes between official curriculum and actual practice.
