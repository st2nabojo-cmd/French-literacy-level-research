## Summary

A book review of *Jinx* (Sage Blackwood, 2013) — a middle grade fantasy novel notable for its moral ambiguity, non-heroic protagonist, and complex supporting cast. The review argues that moral ambiguity is not just tolerable but enriching for the 10–12 age range (middle grade / Tier 4), and that this trend (common in adult science fiction and fantasy) is making appropriate inroads into children's fiction. The protagonist Jinx cannot stand blood or violence; the mentor figure Simon is a condescending workaholic with unclear motives; even the villain provides valid information. For Nabojo, this is a key reference establishing that Tier 4 readers can handle — and are enriched by — genuinely morally complex stories.

## Key Findings / Arguments

1. **Moral ambiguity is a Tier 4 affordance, not a risk.** The reviewer notes that middle grade fiction "still tends to present characters who are either good or bad," but argues that *Jinx* shows this is an unnecessary limitation for the 10–12 age range. The book's complexity is presented as a strength.
2. **The protagonist does not need to be a standard hero.** Jinx is not strong-willed; he fears blood and violence; he is "regularly plagued with doubt and confusion." His conflicted feelings about his mentor (who took care of him but did something terrible) are described as "the sort of realistic conflict that children have about parents."
3. **The mentor figure can be ambiguous.** Simon (the wizard) is "a condescending workaholic who often forgets Jinx is even around." His motives for taking Jinx in are never fully clear. This is presented as a feature, not a flaw.
4. **Even the villain has valid truths.** "Even the Bonemaster has truths to tell, whether or not Jinx wants to hear them." Antagonists can be right about some things.
5. **Humour prevents darkness from becoming grim.** "The book has an off-kilter streak of subtle humour which keeps it from ever getting too grim." This is a craft note for Tier 4 writers: darkness without tonal variation becomes oppressive.
6. **Narrative closure is still needed at this age.** The reviewer criticises the book for leaving too many plotlines unresolved, noting this "makes the book sort of wander." Even Tier 4 readers need structural satisfaction — multiple open plot threads without resolution is a problem.

## Relevant Data

- *Jinx* (Sage Blackwood, 2013): middle grade, ages 10–12+
- Reviewer notes: "Middle grade fiction still tends to present characters who are either good or bad (or occasionally, one pretending to be the other). But Jinx is much more interested in moral ambiguity."
- Comparison drawn to Diana Wynne Jones (noted for complex characters in children's fantasy)

## Tier Implications

### Tier 4 (Matter): Moral Complexity Unlocked
- **Protagonist:** Can be reluctant, doubtful, un-heroic. Does not need to be brave or physically capable. Internal conflict is the engine.
- **Mentor:** Can be morally questionable — withholding information, making poor decisions, or having hidden agendas.
- **Antagonist:** Can make valid arguments; does not need to be purely evil. "The Bonemaster has truths to tell."
- **Moral questions:** Loyalty vs. truth, trust vs. doubt, care vs. control are Tier 4-appropriate.
- **Caution:** Even Tier 4 needs narrative structure and sufficient resolution. Leaving all threads open is unsatisfying; moral complexity must be set within a functioning plot arc.
- **Tone balance:** Mix moral darkness with humour. Sustained grimness without tonal relief is a craft failure at any tier.

> [!warning] This applies to Tier 4 only. The same moral ambiguity in Tier 1–3 characters would be developmentally inappropriate given the Theory of Mind constraints documented in [[theory-of-mind-middle-childhood]].
