## Summary

A NIH-funded experimental psychology study testing working memory development in children aged 7 and 12 versus adults, using auditory sentence lists. The key finding: working memory capacity grows through age by adding more chunk *slots* (not by increasing the amount of information per chunk). 7-year-olds access fewer clauses than older children or adults but recall the same proportion of words from the clauses they do access. This directly constrains how much narrative complexity a Tier 1 reader can hold in mind simultaneously — not because individual sentences are too complex, but because the reader can track fewer simultaneous story elements.

## Key Findings / Arguments

1. **Working memory grows by adding chunk slots, not increasing chunk size.** A "chunk" in linguistic terms is roughly a clause or coherent sentence. 7-year-olds hold fewer chunks than 12-year-olds; 12-year-olds hold fewer than adults.
2. **7-year-olds access fewer clauses but recall them equally well.** Once a chunk is accessed (held in working memory), recall is as complete as for older children. The bottleneck is at capacity (how many), not comprehension (how well).
3. **Adult chunk capacity: ~4–6 chunks.** Classic Miller (1956) "magical number seven" was for augmented memory including rehearsal. True capacity is closer to 3–5 chunks.
4. **Coherent sentences form efficient chunks.** A grammatically and semantically coherent clause is a single chunk. A random word string of the same length counts as multiple chunks. This means: well-structured, grammatical narrative sentences are cognitively easier than poorly structured ones of the same length.
5. **Long sentences (two conjoined clauses) benefit older children more than younger ones.** Younger children do not show the same benefit from the extra structure that conjoining provides — they process the two clauses as two chunks rather than integrating them.

## Relevant Data

- Sample: Children aged 7, children aged 12, adults
- Working memory limit in adults: **~4–6 chunks** (3–5 without rehearsal)
- Key finding: 7-year-olds access **fewer clauses** than 12-year-olds; no difference in **proportion recalled per clause**
- Experimental conditions: 4 short sentences, 8 short sentences, 4 long sentences, 4 random pseudo-sentences

## Tier Implications

### Tier 1 (Manner): Chunk Limit ~3 Simultaneous Story Elements
7-year-old Tier 1 readers have a working memory capacity of approximately 3–4 chunks. For narrative, this translates to:
- **Maximum 3 simultaneous story elements** in play at once (protagonist + one object + one goal, or protagonist + one other character + one problem)
- **Each sentence should be one chunk** — avoid compound sentences that require integrating two clauses into a single meaning unit
- **No running sub-plots** — Tier 1 stories should have one problem, one setting, one goal at a time
- **White space is a cognitive reset** — paragraph breaks allow the reader to release the previous chunk before taking on the next

### Tier 2 (Manner): Growing Chunk Capacity
Tier 2 readers (ages 7–8) are still below adult capacity but growing:
- Can begin handling 4–5 simultaneous elements
- Compound sentences (*Marie voulait aller dehors, mais il pleuvait*) become manageable if used sparingly
- A simple subplot (two separate problems) is becoming possible if one is clearly primary

### Tier 3 (Manner): Approaching Adolescent Capacity
Tier 3 readers (ages 8–10) can hold more elements in working memory:
- Multiple characters with distinct goals and knowledge states
- Compound and complex sentences are fully accessible
- Scene transitions that carry information forward from previous scenes

### Tier 4 (Manner): Near-Adult Chunk Capacity
Tier 4 readers (ages 10–12) are approaching 12-year-old capacity (~adult range):
- Can track layered subplots
- Long, complex sentences with multiple embedded clauses are manageable in moderation

## Limitations / Bias

- Study used auditory sentences (listening), not reading. Reading adds decoding load on top of the chunk capacity constraints, meaning the effective chunk capacity during reading may be even lower than the study suggests — especially for Tier 1 (Chall Stage 1).
- Sentences were in English. French sentence structure (VSO constructions, cleft sentences, more morphological complexity) may alter chunk boundaries differently.
- 7-year-olds were the youngest group; no direct data on 6-year-olds (Tier 1 onset).
