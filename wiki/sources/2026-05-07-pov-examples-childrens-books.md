## Summary

A teaching article cataloguing picture book and chapter book examples for each of the three narrative points of view (1st, 2nd, 3rd person), with examples organised by age and format. For Nabojo, the critical finding is the explicit link between **second person ("you") narration** and the choose-your-own-adventure format — Nabojo's core interactive mechanic. The article confirms that 2nd person POV is used across ages but is particularly natural for branching/interactive stories because the reader is the protagonist. The article also provides a clean taxonomy of POV types with age-graded examples.

## Key Findings / Arguments

1. **Second person POV = interactive/choose-your-own-adventure.** The article explicitly links 2nd person ("you decide," "you go to...") to branching narrative. Examples: *Princess Island* ("If you decide to take the first boat, turn to page 10"), *Escape from a Video Game* ("You are the hero"). This is Nabojo's default choice-point framing voice.
2. **First person is the most emotionally accessible.** "I, me, us, ours" — the narrator is typically the protagonist. Natural for young readers because it matches their own inner monologue. Strong emotional immediacy.
3. **Third person omniscient enables dramatic irony.** The narrator can reveal things no character knows. This is a Tier 3–4 technique because it requires the reader to track the gap between narrator knowledge and character knowledge (AToM capability).
4. **Third person limited follows one character's perspective** without omniscience. Good for Tier 2–3: emotionally connected but without the complexity of full omniscience.
5. **POV ≠ perspective.** POV is the grammatical voice (who narrates). Perspective is the worldview through which events are filtered. Multiple characters can narrate in 3rd person limited, creating shifting perspectives — a Tier 4 technique.
6. **Age-format correlation from the examples:**
   - Picture books + early readers (ages 5–8): 1st and 2nd person most common
   - Chapter books (ages 8–12): All three, with 1st person still dominant; 3rd person omniscient appears

## Relevant Data

- 2nd person examples span ages 5–12: picture books (*Good Eating: The Short Life of Krill*, *King of Kindergarten*) to chapter books (*Escape from a Video Game*, ages 8–12)
- Choose-your-own-adventure format cited: *Princess Island* (2nd person), *Escape from a Video Game* (2nd person)
- First person examples across all ages from picture books to chapter books (ages 6–12+)

## Tier Implications

### Tier 1 (Matter + Manner): First or Second Person
- **Default narrative POV:** First person (I/me) for main story; second person for interactive choice points
- Avoid third person omniscient — requires tracking narrator vs. character knowledge (AToM demand)
- Third person limited is accessible (ages 6–7 can follow "Leo went to the forest") but less emotionally immediate

### Tier 2 (Matter + Manner): Expanding POV Options
- First and second person remain primary
- Third person limited becomes comfortable — single character perspective
- The reader can begin tracking simple dramatic irony ("we know the cat is hiding but the boy doesn't")

### Tier 3 (Matter + Manner): Third Person Omniscient Accessible
- Third person omniscient opens up — the narrator can know things no character knows
- Shifting perspectives (two characters each narrating in limited 3rd) manageable
- 2nd person for interactive choice points continues to work

### Tier 4 (Matter + Manner): Full POV Range
- Unreliable narrator (1st person who is wrong or deceiving) becomes possible
- Multiple shifting perspectives explicitly compared and contrasted
- 2nd person can be used for immersive, high-stakes choices

### All Tiers (Interactive Choice Mechanics)
> [!pipeline]
> **Choice point framing voice:** Second person ("Tu décides de...", "Si tu veux aller au marché, tourne à la page 10"). This is the standard Nabojo interactive choice framing and should be consistent across all tiers.

## Limitations / Bias

- Teaching article with curated examples, not research. POV recommendations are based on classroom practice and editorial convention, not developmental studies.
- All examples are Anglophone. French children's literature POV conventions may differ (French literary tradition has strong 3rd person narrator conventions).
