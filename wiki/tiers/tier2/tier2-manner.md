## Tier Writing Profile

Tier 2 readers (ages 7–8, CE1 and early CE2) are in Chall's Stage 2: the fluency-building stage. Decoding is no longer the bottleneck — most words are sight-read — but automaticity is not yet fully consolidated. The critical Chall insight: Stage 2 reading is **not** for encountering new information. It is for **confirming what is already known**. Unfamiliar vocabulary, settings, or conceptual content directly competes with the fluency-building process. Working memory has grown — Tier 2 readers can hold 4–5 chunks simultaneously — allowing compound sentences with one subordinate clause and the tracking of 4–5 distinct story elements (characters, locations, objects). The AToM milestone (~age 7) is recently crossed: simple deception, surprise, and secrets are now comprehensible. Silent reading begins to replace read-aloud in CE1; CE2 readers may be reading independently for the first time. Nabojo's Tier 2 writing must feel like a fluency reward: smooth, predictable, slightly more complex than the reader expects of themselves, pleasurably fast.

## Sentence Structure

| Dimension | Guideline | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Average sentence length | 8–13 words | Within the 4–5 chunk budget; allows one additional clause over Tier 1 |
| Maximum sentence length | 20 words | Above this, clause boundaries become ambiguous under partial fluency |
| Subordinate clause nesting | Max 1 level | One embedded clause is manageable; nesting two creates parsing failure |
| Preferred construction | SVO with optional adverbial or single subordinate | `Quand Léa arriva, elle vit le chat.` — single temporal clause permitted |
| Coordinating conjunctions | `et`, `mais`, `alors`, `donc`, `car` | All coordinating conjunctions now accessible; `or` (French) rare but permitted |
| Dialogue tags | Varied but simple | `dit`, `cria`, `demanda`, `répondit`, `chuchota` — introduce tag variety gradually |

**Permitted constructions:**
- `Quand il entendit le bruit, il s'arrêta.` — temporal subordinate (single level)
- `Léa cherchait son chat, mais il avait disparu.` — coordinated contrast
- `Elle savait que la forêt était dangereuse.` — single `que`-complement clause
- `C'est là qu'elle aperçut la lumière.` — presentative + relative (simple)

**Prohibited constructions:**
- Double embedding: *`Elle pensa que si Leo arrivait, tout irait bien.`*
- Participial clauses: *`Arrivant dans la salle, elle remarqua...`*
- Indirect speech with tense shift: *`Il dit qu'il était allé hier...`* — tense backshifting is Stage 3+
- Complex relative clauses with `dont` or `auquel`: accessible in oral French but parsing cost is high in early-stage reading

## Vocabulary Level

```yaml
vocabulary:
  base_register: A1-A2
  manulex_grade: G2
  manulex_lemma_pool: 10400
  max_new_words_per_page: 3
  new_word_introduction: always in context with semantic support (synonym or action that demonstrates meaning)
  avoid:
    - words absent from MANULEX G2 without contextual scaffolding
    - abstract nouns as primary story-driving vocabulary
    - domain-specific technical vocabulary (science, history terms) as plot-essential words
    - anglicismes and register shifts (argot in narrator voice)
  favour:
    - familiar G1 words supplemented by select G2 expansion
    - emotionally resonant concrete vocabulary: rire, trembler, chercher, trouver, s'enfuir
    - sensory and descriptive adjectives within the familiar world: sombre, brillant, doux, effrayant
    - interpersonal verbs: promettre, mentir, deviner, espérer (newly accessible with AToM milestone)
```

"New word" at Tier 2 means any word not in the reader's active oral vocabulary — typically, words outside MANULEX G2 high-dispersion or words used in an unfamiliar sense. Three per page is the ceiling; the new word must be introduceable through the surrounding context without reader intervention. At this tier, a character can *deviner* something (guess) without the word needing explicit definition — the action makes meaning. This contextual inference is the beginning of vocabulary acquisition through reading, which will be the primary mechanism in Stage 3 and beyond.

## Narrative Pacing

- **Chapter/scene length:** 600–1,000 words per chapter. A Tier 2 chapter is a complete emotional arc: setup, complication, partial resolution (or an unresolved hook). The reader completes a chapter in one sitting; this is the primary unit of engagement.
- **Full story length:** 5,000–8,700 words. Equivalent to a transitional chapter book (~10 chapters of 600–870 words). This is the upper limit before multi-session investment risks dropout for this age group.
- **Beats per scene:** 2–3. More emotional complexity is now tolerable, but each scene still has a primary beat and at most one complication or sub-beat.
- **Cliffhangers:** Yes, at chapter endings. A Tier 2 cliffhanger is a concrete unresolved action (`Mais alors, quelqu'un frappa à la porte.`) not a philosophical question. The hook must be resolvable in the first 2–3 sentences of the next chapter.
- **White space:** Paragraphs of 3–5 sentences are now appropriate. Dialogue remains its own paragraph. Scene transitions may use a visual break (blank line) without an explicit transition sentence.
- **Exposition ratio:** Maximum 40% of any chapter can be pure description or reflection. Action must anchor every major passage.
- **Chapter length consistency:** Chapters should be approximately equal in length — irregularity in pacing is a Tier 3+ technique. Tier 2 readers develop a pace expectation from the first chapter; violating it mid-book creates difficulty.

## Grammar Rules and Constraints

- **Tense:** Présent de l'indicatif remains the primary narrative voice. Passé composé in dialogue and for past events referenced by characters (`"Il a pris mon vélo !"`). Imparfait is now accessible as a secondary narrative tense for states and descriptions, but passé composé remains primary for action sequences. Futur simple (`il arrivera`) may appear in dialogue and thought (`Elle espérait qu'il arriverait`).
- **Passive voice:** Use sparingly. A passive construction is only acceptable when the agent is genuinely unknown or unimportant (`La porte avait été laissée ouverte.`). Prefer active for all action sequences.
- **Negation:** `ne...pas`, `ne...plus`, `ne...jamais` all accessible. `ne...rien` and `ne...personne` as subject are now appropriate (`Personne ne répondit.`).
- **Relative clauses:** `qui` and `que` clauses permitted at one level (`le chat qui dormait`, `la chose qu'elle voulait`). `dont` and `où` accessible but use sparingly.
- **Register — dialogue:** `tu` between peers and with familiar adults remains the norm. `Vous` for formal adult characters (a teacher, a stranger, a shopkeeper) is appropriate and culturally correct for France. Mix is acceptable when reflecting real French social register.
- **Free indirect speech (style indirect libre):** Beginning to be usable — a brief dip into the protagonist's internal voice without an explicit `il pensa que` marker. Use in single sentences only; do not sustain for more than 2–3 consecutive sentences.

## Engagement Techniques

- **Direct address:** Reserved for choice points. *`Et toi, qu'est-ce que tu ferais à la place de Léa ?`* — this phrasing models the choice-point template. In non-choice narrative, direct address breaks immersion.
- **Repetition with variation:** Accumulative structures remain powerful but now serve a different function — building narrative momentum rather than vocabulary repetition. A pattern repeated across chapters with a surprising break at the climax is a satisfying Tier 2 technique.
- **Humour type:** Situational and perspective-based humour now works reliably (AToM milestone reached). A character can believe something false that the reader knows is false — comic irony is now accessible. Wordplay at a concrete level (homophones, simple puns) works when it does not require sustained metalinguistic reflection.
- **Tension mechanics:** Tension can now be sustained across multiple scenes (unlike Tier 1). The obstacle-and-delay structure works: present the problem, have the protagonist try and fail, try again, succeed. Do not sustain unresolved tension for more than 2–3 chapters without relief.
- **Foreshadowing:** Simple, one-step foreshadowing is now effective. Plant a concrete detail; pay it off within the same book. Do not plant clues that require remembering details from more than 3 chapters earlier.
- **Emotional mirroring:** A brief sentence reflecting the protagonist's interior state at each emotional peak keeps the reader emotionally anchored. *`Léa sentit son cœur battre fort.`* — sensory-emotional, no abstract vocabulary.

## Interactive Choice Mechanics

> [!pipeline]
> **Tier 2 interactive choice specification:**
>
> - **Choice point frequency:** Once every 2–3 chapters. At chapter length (600–1,000 words), this means a reader encounters 3–5 choices per story — enough to feel agency without fragmenting narrative immersion.
> - **Number of choices per point:** 2–3. Two choices remain the default; a third is introduced only when a genuine three-way situation exists naturally in the story. Never add a third choice for variety — working memory is still limited.
> - **Choice framing language (French templates):**
>   - `Tu décides pour Léa : est-ce qu'elle parle à l'inconnu, ou est-ce qu'elle part en courant ?`
>   - `Qu'est-ce que tu ferais à sa place ? Tu peux choisir : aider le chien, ou rentrer chez toi.`
>   - `La décision appartient à Léo. Toi, tu choisis : il monte dans l'arbre, ou il reste sur le chemin ?`
> - **Voice at choice points:** 2nd person (`tu`) for the choice framing. Return to 3rd person narration immediately after.
> - **Choice consequence weight:** Moderate. The choice changes the next 1–2 scenes (different characters met, different obstacles encountered) but the story rejoins at the same structural beat 2–3 scenes later. Consequences are concrete and narratively satisfying without creating fully divergent story branches that the reader must track.
> - **Prohibited choice types:**
>   - Choices between morally equivalent options with no narrative differentiation (both lead immediately to the same outcome)
>   - Choices with a "trap" option that results in the protagonist failing or suffering in ways that are hard to exit
>   - Choices that require knowledge the reader is unlikely to have (historical, cultural, technical)

## What to Avoid

- **New conceptual domains as story engine:** Introducing an unfamiliar world, period, or scientific domain as the story's primary setting adds cognitive load that blocks fluency. Keep settings familiar even when extending narrative complexity.
- **Tense inconsistency:** Mixing présent and passé composé within the same action sequence confuses temporal orientation. Commit to one primary tense per scene and change intentionally at scene transitions.
- **Deep interior monologue:** Extended first-person-style reflection embedded in 3rd person narration requires a cognitive stance-shift Tier 2 readers are not yet reliable at. Keep interior access brief (1–2 sentences) and clearly attributable.
- **Sentences opening with long adverbial phrases:** *`Dans la lumière dorée du soir, juste avant que le soleil disparaisse, Léa...`* — front-loading delays the subject and verb. Keep the subject within the first 5 words of any sentence.
- **Multiple antagonists introduced simultaneously:** Two obstacles presented in the same scene create tracking confusion. Introduce complications sequentially.
- **Historical or technical vocabulary as story-essential:** If the reader must know what a `donjon` or a `chlorophylle` is to follow the plot, the word is over-weight for Tier 2. Either use familiar synonyms or defer the topic to Tier 3+.

## Open Questions / Research Gaps

> [!question] Is the Chall Stage 2 "familiar content" constraint validated specifically for French CE1/CE2 readers? All Chall research was conducted in English. French has more orthographically complex decoding rules than English — a French CE1 reader may still be in a semi-decoding state even when English counterparts have reached full automaticity. This may require even stronger familiar-content constraints at Tier 2 in French.

> [!question] What is the optimal choice-point frequency for Tier 2? The 2–3 chapter spacing above is inferred from working memory constraints, not from empirical data on Tier 2 engagement with interactive fiction. Research on branching narrative comprehension at ages 7–8 would allow this to be calibrated.

> [!question] At what point does free indirect speech become comprehensible and pleasurable for Tier 2 readers? One sentence of FIS is assumed to be accessible; sustained FIS is not. No French-language data on this specific reading comprehension milestone was found.
