## Tier Overview

Tier 2 readers (ages 7–8, CE1 and early CE2) are in Chall's Stage 2: the fluency-building stage. The key insight from Chall — easily missed by content producers — is that Stage 2 reading is **not** for encountering new information; it is for **confirming what is already known**. Readers at this stage build speed and confidence by processing familiar content. This means Tier 2 stories must keep *settings and concepts* familiar even as they can extend *narrative structure* and *character complexity*. Simultaneously, most Tier 2 readers have recently crossed the Theory of Mind milestone (~age 7): they can now understand that characters hold beliefs that differ from reality, enabling simple secrets, surprises, and light deception in stories. Working memory is growing — Tier 2 readers can track 4–5 simultaneous story elements and begin following a character across multiple scenes. In the French school system, CE1 is when silent reading begins to displace read-aloud; CE2 marks the beginning of autonomous reading for pleasure. Nabojo's Tier 2 content sits at this transition.

## Permitted Topics

- **Familiar settings with adventure** — school, home, village, park with a complication (a mystery in the classroom, a lost pet in the garden, a problem at the *marché*); familiar setting removes conceptual load so the reader can enjoy narrative tension
- **Simple mysteries and small investigations** — who took the biscuit? where did the rabbit go? age-appropriate epistemology (looking for clues, asking questions, testing hypotheses) — cognitively engaging without complexity
- **Friendship challenges** — a disagreement with a friend, a new child joining the class; social navigation now includes mild deception and perspective-taking
- **Animal stories with personality** — animals who have distinct personalities, secrets, or small agendas; the AToM milestone means animals can now have inner lives readers track
- **Light fantasy with clear rules** — a small magical creature, a wish with consequences, a magic object that does one specific thing; the "rules" of the fantasy must be explained early and followed consistently
- **Short journeys within the familiar world** — a day trip, a visit to a relative's village, a first sleepover; slightly extending the familiar world without going fully into the unknown
- **Beginning of gentle humour with perspective** — "the frog thought the bucket was the whole world" type humour, which requires simple perspective-taking

## Topics to Avoid

- **Entirely unfamiliar world-building** — fantasy worlds that require the reader to learn new geography, rules, and social structures simultaneously (too much cognitive load for Tier 2's fluency priority)
- **Deep moral ambiguity** — characters who are meaningfully neither good nor bad; Tier 2's AToM is newly acquired and cannot yet hold "the antagonist had a valid point"
- **Sustained suspense without resolution** — brief cliff-hangers are possible (end of chapter), but sustained anxiety across many scenes is too heavy for this tier
- **Complex family dynamics requiring backstory** — estrangement, trauma, illness as plot driver; not because these don't exist, but because Tier 2 narrative must be resolved and tight
- **Informational novelty as the story's engine** — Chall Stage 2 constraint: introducing new knowledge domains competes with fluency building
- **Multiple simultaneous antagonists** — one clear obstacle/problem at a time

## Narrative Types

- **Simple mystery arc** — problem posed, clues gathered, solution found; highly motivating for this age; works within familiar settings
- **Friendship arc** — conflict → misunderstanding → repair; the AToM milestone makes the misunderstanding legible now
- **Extended problem–solution** — 3–5 obstacles before the final solution; slightly more complex than Tier 1's single-step problem
- **Beginning Hero's Journey** — Departure (something triggers the quest) → simple tests and helpers → Return (with reward); Acts 1 and 3 with simplified Act 2
- **Comic situation** — a funny misunderstanding, a character who makes increasingly wrong choices before a comic resolution; humour is a strong Tier 2 engagement tool

## Character Archetypes

- **Child protagonist with 2–3 clear traits** — slightly more complex than Tier 1; can have contradictory traits (brave but also scared; kind but also impatient)
- **Friend with contrasting personality** — introduces perspective difference; the protagonist and friend can disagree and reconcile
- **Simple mentor or helpful adult** — parent, grandparent, librarian; provides information or a tool, but the protagonist solves the problem
- **Obstacle figure with understandable motivation** — "the dog is guarding the garden because he's afraid" — now the motivation is stated or shown, not just the behaviour
- **Optional Trickster** — a funny, mischief-making character (animal or child); adds humour and plot complexity without deepening moral ambiguity
- **Maximum 5 named characters** (working memory constraint, growing from Tier 1's 3)

## Emotional and Moral Range

- **Emotions accessible:** Full Tier 1 range plus: mild jealousy (resolved), simple shame (overcome), genuine surprise and delight, anticipation, simple disappointment resolved through effort
- **Emotions to avoid:** Sustained shame, unresolved jealousy, grief as a central unresolved theme, rage
- **Moral complexity:** Simple right/wrong remains the baseline, but a character can make a wrong choice for an understandable reason — and must recognise and correct it
- **New at Tier 2:** Simple deception is now comprehensible ("Marie didn't tell Leo the surprise was a party") — but deceit must be either benign (surprise party) or recognised and corrected
- **Taboo topics:** Same as Tier 1; moral ambiguity that leaves the reader uncertain about what was right is still inappropriate

## French Cultural Considerations

- **CE1/CE2 school milestones:** Learning multiplication, first history lessons, silent reading begins; stories can reference these as background without requiring explanation
- **Cultural touchstones:** *La bibliothèque scolaire* (school library), *le club de sport* (after-school sport), *les cousins, les vacances chez les grands-parents* — familiar extended-family contexts
- **French nature and geography:** *La forêt, la rivière, le jardin, la ferme* — rural France accessible from urban contexts; Tier 2 readers from Paris and those from the Dordogne share these
- **French animal fable tradition:** Tier 2 is the age when children encounter La Fontaine's fables at school — animal-with-personality stories resonate with this educational context
- **Avoid:** Complex French historical or political references; regional dialects without gloss; Anglophone cultural references that would confuse a French child
