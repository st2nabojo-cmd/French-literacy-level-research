## Tier Writing Profile

Tier 4 readers (ages 10–12, CM1, CM2, and 6ème) are at the upper boundary of Chall's Stage 3 and beginning to approach Stage 4. They read fluently across a wide vocabulary range, process complex sentence structures without cognitive strain, and can track multiple story threads across an extended narrative. Working memory is near-adult (~6–7 chunks), enabling sentences with multiple embedded clauses, large cast tracking, and sustained engagement across a full middle-grade novel with subplots, time shifts, and an extended timeline. Theory of Mind is fully adult-equivalent across all three AToM factors: Tier 4 readers track complex deception, appreciate dramatic irony effortlessly, and can sustain interest in characters whose motivations remain genuinely uncertain across an entire book. The transition to 6ème (*le collège*) is a major life event that coincides with this tier — stories that engage with identity, belonging, and moral agency resonate deeply at this age. Writing for Tier 4 is not about constraint-management but about craft: the reader can handle almost any formal technique; the question is whether the technique serves the story.

## Sentence Structure

| Dimension | Guideline | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Average sentence length | 15–25 words | Near-adult processing capacity; rhythm and variety matter more than length caps |
| Maximum sentence length | 40 words | Long sentences are appropriate when the content demands it; clarity remains the constraint |
| Subordinate clause nesting | Max 3 levels | Beyond 3 levels of embedding, even adult readers require re-reading |
| Preferred construction | Varied for rhythm | Sentence length and structure variation is a craft choice, not a constraint |
| Participial phrases | Fully accessible | Fronted participials, appositive phrases, absolute constructions — all in range |
| Free indirect speech | Fully accessible | Extended interior passages without attribution; sustained for several paragraphs |

**Permitted constructions:**
- Full passé simple literary narrative: `Il aperçut, au fond de la cour, une silhouette qu'il reconnut aussitôt.`
- Appositive constructions: `Léa, la seule à savoir la vérité, garda le silence.`
- Complex subordination: `Même si elle savait que ce choix était irréversible, elle n'hésita pas un instant.`
- Inversions for literary effect: `Jamais il n'avait vu quelque chose de pareil.` — emphatic negative inversion
- Extended FIS passages: multiple consecutive sentences of interior voice without attribution
- Imparfait de narration (imparfait pittoresque): for vivid scene-setting (`La pluie tombait sans relâche. Les rues étaient vides. Quelque chose clochait.`)

**Note on stylistic variation:** At Tier 4, the goal is not to avoid complex structures but to use them with intention. A sentence that is long because the thought is complex is appropriate. A sentence that is long because it has not been revised is not. Craft over constraint.

## Vocabulary Level

```yaml
vocabulary:
  base_register: B1-B2
  manulex_grade: G3-5 full
  manulex_lemma_pool: 22411
  max_new_words_per_page: 8
  new_word_introduction: contextual inference primary; dictionary-lookup occasional (word must be memorable enough to be worth that investment)
  avoid:
    - gratuitous register elevation (vocabulary chosen to sound literary rather than to serve meaning)
    - adult jargon from specialised domains (legal, medical, academic) without narrative grounding
    - anglicismes in narrator voice (acceptable in contemporary dialogue for atmosphere)
  favour:
    - full G3-5 MANULEX range including literary and historical vocabulary
    - psychological and moral vocabulary: trahison, ambiguïté, désillusion, résilience, conviction
    - abstract connectors for argument and nuance: néanmoins, par conséquent, en dépit de, à cet égard
    - precise sensory and atmospheric vocabulary: crépusculaire, sourd (as in bruit sourd), oppressant
    - register-marking vocabulary that signals character voice (dialect markers, sociolect, formal/informal contrast)
```

Tier 4 is the first tier where vocabulary acquisition through reading operates at full efficiency — the reader infers, retains, and reuses new words encountered in context without requiring explicit instruction. Up to 8 new words per page is calibrated on the assumption that the reader can infer most of them from context without breaking reading flow. Words that are neither familiar nor inferable from context should still be avoided unless they are definitionally central (a key term in a historical or fantasy context). High-quality literary vocabulary — words that are precise and pleasurable — is an engagement feature at Tier 4, not a burden.

## Narrative Pacing

- **Chapter length:** 2,000–3,500 words. Tier 4 readers invest in multi-session reading across days or weeks. Chapter length can vary by function: an action chapter may be 1,500 words; an emotionally resonant chapter may run 4,000. Consistent average length matters more than individual chapter uniformity.
- **Full story length:** 30,000–60,000 words. Middle-grade novel range. This is the Tier 4 norm.
- **Beats per scene:** 3–5. A scene at Tier 4 can sustain emotional complexity: more than one thing can happen internally or externally within a single scene.
- **Cliffhangers:** Varied. Chapter hooks at Tier 4 include: revelation, reversal, character decision with delayed consequence, a new character or information that recontextualises what preceded it. Not every chapter ends with action — some end with a character realisation.
- **White space:** Flexible. Dense paragraphs for interior monologue and reflection; shorter paragraphs for action and tension. The prose rhythm is a narrative signal: slower prose = contemplation; faster/shorter = urgency.
- **Exposition ratio:** No hard ceiling. A chapter may be predominantly atmospheric or reflective if it serves character development. The constraint is engagement, not percentage.
- **Subplots:** 2–3 active subplots are manageable. Each needs a distinct beat structure and a clear connection to the main arc. Subplots should pay off before the final act.
- **Dual or multiple timelines:** Accessible. Alternating past/present chapters, or brief flashbacks, are fully manageable. Temporal orientation must be clear (visual break + first-sentence anchor), but the mechanism does not need to be explained to the reader.

## Grammar Rules and Constraints

- **Tense:** Passé simple + imparfait as the standard literary pair. Passé simple is now fully consolidated at CM2/6ème. Présent de narration (dramatic present) usable strategically for climactic moments. Flashbacks may use plus-que-parfait. Futur antérieur accessible for conditional sequences in dialogue.
- **Passive voice:** Used purposefully for narrative effect — focus shift, agent suppression, formal register. Overuse remains a stylistic flaw.
- **Subjunctive:** Fully accessible including passé du subjonctif in formal and literary registers. Subjunctive in main clauses for literary effect (`Qu'il parte ou non, la décision était prise.`).
- **Negation:** All forms accessible. Formal inversions (`N'eût-il pas...`, literary conditional) are comprehensible but should be used sparingly and only when the register demands it.
- **Register:** Multiple character registers in a single text are fully manageable and add depth. A character's register is a characterisation tool. Narrator register should remain consistent (or shift consistently with a narrative device such as FIS).
- **Style indirect libre (FIS):** A primary Tier 4 literary tool. Sustained FIS is the mechanism for deep third-person characterisation. The reader is expected to track whose interior voice is speaking without markers.
- **Inversion for emphasis:** Stylistic inversion (`Rarement avait-il vu...`, `À peine était-il entré...`) adds literary texture and is now accessible. Use sparingly to preserve effect.

## Engagement Techniques

- **Dramatic irony at scale:** The reader can carry privileged knowledge across an entire novel — knowing the villain's true identity from chapter 2 while the protagonist discovers it in chapter 18. This long-form dramatic irony is a Tier 4 exclusive engagement technique.
- **Unreliable narrator signals:** A narrator who gives subtle indications of bias, omission, or self-deception is now comprehensible. The reader at Tier 4 can detect unreliability from inconsistency and misdirection, even without explicit marking.
- **Humour:** Irony, satire, dark comedy, and wit in dialogue all accessible. Humour at Tier 4 can coexist with serious stakes — in fact, a book that never lightens is harder to sustain than one with strategic levity.
- **Moral ambiguity as engagement:** The reader at Tier 4 is capable of genuine uncertainty about what a character should do, and of sustaining that uncertainty without resolution anxiety. This is a new engagement mechanism — the reader reads on not to find the "right answer" but to see how the author handles an irreducible dilemma.
- **Tension mechanics:** Tension at Tier 4 can operate on multiple levels simultaneously: physical danger, social stakes, moral stakes, and emotional stakes can all be active in the same chapter. Alternating resolution on one dimension while escalating another is the mark of sophisticated middle-grade craft.
- **The revelation chapter:** At Tier 4, a chapter whose entire function is to reveal something already long foreshadowed — changing the reader's understanding of everything that preceded it — is a powerful standalone technique. Structure the first third of the book to enable this.

## Interactive Choice Mechanics

> [!pipeline]
> **Tier 4 interactive choice specification:**
>
> - **Choice point frequency:** Once every 3–4 chapters (approximately every 8,000–12,000 words). At Tier 4 chapter lengths, this means 4–6 major choices in a full-length story. Choices should be placed at genuine narrative turning points — after a crisis, before a major commitment, at a moment of moral decision.
> - **Number of choices per point:** 3. Three options reflecting genuinely different character philosophies or strategies. At Tier 4, choices can reflect moral stances, not just action options: the three paths might be "confront directly," "deceive to protect," "do nothing and wait."
> - **Choice framing language (French templates):**
>   - `[Protagonist] est au carrefour. Toi, tu choisis sa voie : [Option A] | [Option B] | [Option C]`
>   - `Le moment est décisif. Qu'est-ce que [protagonist] va faire ?`
>   - `Trois chemins. Trois versions de [protagonist]. Laquelle choisiras-tu ?`
> - **Voice at choice points:** The narrator addresses the reader in a tone consistent with the book's overall register — literary, not game-like. Tier 4 readers are aware they are inside a narrative construct; the choice-point voice can acknowledge this lightly (`C'est ton tour de choisir`) without breaking immersion destructively.
> - **Choice consequence weight:** Significant to major. The reader's choice should generate a meaningfully different 4–6 chapter sequence — different relationships deepened, different information accessed, different costs incurred — before rejoining the main arc at the final act's opening. At Tier 4, the reader should feel that their version of the story is genuinely different from the version someone who made a different choice read.
> - **Moral consequence weight:** At Tier 4, a choice can lead to a genuinely morally mixed outcome. Choosing the "brave" option may result in someone getting hurt. Choosing the "safe" option may leave an injustice unaddressed. The story does not punish the reader for their choice, but it does not erase consequences.
> - **Prohibited choice types:**
>   - Choices where all three options lead to the same immediate outcome (reader will notice and disengage)
>   - Choices that require background knowledge uniquely available to one path (unfair asymmetry)
>   - Choices that generate such severe permanent divergence that the reader's investment in "the other path" undermines their engagement with their own

## What to Avoid

- **Flat moral resolution:** A Tier 4 story that ends with all ambiguity neatly resolved and a clear moral lesson delivered is underwritten for this tier. The reader is capable of leaving a story without every question answered; denying them that sophistication is a wasted opportunity.
- **Unearned complexity:** Introducing multiple POVs, unreliable narrators, or complex timelines as style without serving the story's meaning. Complexity at Tier 4 is a tool, not a marker of quality.
- **Adult themes without narrative containment:** Tier 4 can engage with dark topics (historical violence, family loss, systemic injustice), but the protagonist's emotional journey through those topics must be the story's spine. Dark content that exists without a character arc to contain it crosses the line.
- **Irony without anchor:** Sustained ironic detachment in a narrator can feel cold. Even at Tier 4, the reader must be given emotional access points — characters they care about, stakes they understand — or the irony reads as indifference.
- **Over-long explanatory passages:** Tier 4 readers infer. A passage of three sentences explaining the thematic significance of an event will feel condescending. Show the event; trust the reader.
- **Cliffhangers at the expense of earned resolution:** Ending every chapter on a pure action hook without any scene that breathes or consolidates creates exhaustion rather than momentum. Alternate high-tension chapter endings with quieter, character-building endings.
