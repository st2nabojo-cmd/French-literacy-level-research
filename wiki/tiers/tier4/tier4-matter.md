## Tier Overview

Tier 4 readers (ages 10–12, CM1, CM2, and 6ème) are at the upper boundary of Chall's Stage 3 and beginning to approach Stage 4. They read to learn, but they are now also capable of encountering — and being enriched by — moral complexity, ambiguity, and multiple viewpoints. Theory of Mind at this age approaches full competence across all three factors (social reasoning, reasoning about ambiguity, naturalistic social interpretation): readers can track complex deception, dramatic irony, and characters whose motivations are genuinely uncertain. Working memory is near-adult; Tier 4 readers can sustain interest across a full middle-grade novel with multiple subplots, a large cast, and an extended timeline. In the French school system, CM2 and 6ème represent the transition from primary to secondary school (*le collège*) — an emotionally significant transition that good Tier 4 stories can engage with directly. This is the age of *préadolescence*: identity formation, peer relationships with real stakes, and the beginning of genuine moral agency in the world.

## Permitted Topics

- **Complex adventure with moral stakes** — a quest where the right choice is not obvious, or where doing the right thing has real costs
- **Social and political dynamics** — school politics, social hierarchies, loyalty vs. fairness; the beginning of understanding that systems can be unjust
- **Fantasy and science fiction with world-building depth** — invented worlds with their own histories, factions, and rules; the reader can now sustain and enjoy this complexity
- **Historical events with moral complexity** — *la Résistance, l'esclavage, la colonisation* — French and Francophone history at its most challenging is now accessible; must be handled with care but not avoided
- **Identity and belonging** — who am I? where do I belong? what do I believe? — the defining questions of préadolescence; can be the emotional core of a Tier 4 story
- **Genuine moral dilemmas** — a character must choose between two values (loyalty vs. truth; safety vs. justice); the story can leave the reader uncertain about whether the choice was right
- **Unreliable or complex authority** — adults can be wrong, misguided, or acting from self-interest; this is now comprehensible and produces rich narrative tension
- **Light romantic content** — early friendship-becoming-more, a first crush; no explicit content; age-appropriate emotional complexity only

## Topics to Avoid

- **Graphic violence with detailed suffering** — action violence in a fantasy or adventure context is fine; torture, gore, or suffering for its own sake is not
- **Sexual content** — beyond age-appropriate early romantic feeling; Nabojo serves a 6–12 audience and this tier overlaps with 12-year-olds who must be protected
- **Nihilism or hopelessness without counterpoint** — Tier 4 can be dark, but darkness must exist in tension with meaning; a story that provides no resolution or hope crosses the line
- **Adult addiction, abuse, or trauma as central themes requiring adult processing** — these can appear as background facts but should not be the unresolved emotional core
- **Pure shock value or darkness for atmosphere** — moral complexity in service of character and story; not moral ambiguity as a stylistic pose

## Narrative Types

- **Full Hero's Journey with subversion** — the reluctant hero, the anti-hero, the hero who fails and must try again; all variations accessible
- **Multi-character ensemble** — four or five protagonists each with distinct arcs that intersect; requires and rewards sustained engagement
- **Moral dilemma arc** — a character faces an impossible choice; both paths have real costs; the story explores consequences without prescribing a verdict
- **Historical immersion** — child or young adult protagonist in a period setting navigating real historical forces; the protagonist's choices matter even if history is fixed
- **Mystery with moral dimension** — not just "whodunit" but "what should I do with what I know?"
- **Coming-of-age arc** — the character enters the story as one version of themselves and exits as another; internal transformation is the story's true subject

## Character Archetypes

- **Morally complex protagonist** — not consistently brave or good; capable of cowardice, selfishness, poor judgment; growth is earned and non-linear
- **Ambiguous mentor** — a guide whose motivations are not fully transparent; the protagonist must learn to rely on themselves
- **Antagonist with a valid argument** — the "villain" is right about some things; their position has internal logic; their methods are wrong but their analysis may not be (cf. *Jinx*'s Bonemaster)
- **Shape Shifter with unresolved allegiance** — a character the reader genuinely does not know whether to trust until late in the story
- **Mirror character** — a character who represents what the protagonist could become (good version or bad version of the same essential self)
- **No character count ceiling** — adult working memory is approaching; large ensembles are manageable if well-differentiated

## Emotional and Moral Range

- **Emotions accessible:** Full emotional range including moral outrage, genuine grief (with processing, not resolution per se), ambivalent love, existential uncertainty, righteous anger, compassion for an opponent
- **Emotions to avoid:** Sexual desire (beyond early romantic feeling), contempt without narrative consequence
- **Moral complexity:** Genuine moral dilemmas where the "right" answer is contested; characters who do wrong for good reasons and good things for wrong reasons; villains with valid grievances
- **New at Tier 4:** The reader can leave a story without every question answered, provided the emotional arc is complete. The protagonist can be changed without being fully resolved.
- **Taboo topics:** Graphic sexual content, torture, exploitation of children within the story world

> [!pipeline]
> Tier 4 emotional contract: the story must provide emotional satisfaction and meaningful stakes — but it does not need to resolve every moral question. Ambiguity is an acceptable ending if the protagonist's journey is complete.

## French Cultural Considerations

- **La transition collège (6ème)** — the move from primary to secondary school is a major cultural and emotional event; stories set at this threshold resonate deeply for CM2/6ème readers
- **La Résistance, la Shoah, la décolonisation** — French history curriculum at CM2/6ème explicitly addresses these; stories touching on them are curriculum-resonant and emotionally powerful
- **Francophone identity** — Tier 4 is old enough to engage with what it means to be French, or Francophone, or to have a family from elsewhere; immigration, mixed identity, and cultural belonging are live topics
- **La banlieue, le collège de quartier** — urban realities that are part of many French children's lives; Tier 4 is old enough to see them as story settings, not just backgrounds
- **French literary tradition:** *Le Petit Prince* (Saint-Exupéry) is typically read at this age; moral registers of *L'Ami retrouvé*, *Manon des sources* themes are entering cultural consciousness
