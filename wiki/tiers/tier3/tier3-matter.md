## Tier Overview

Tier 3 readers (ages 8–10, CE2 and CM1) are transitioning into Chall's Stage 3: reading to learn. For the first time, a story can introduce genuinely new knowledge, settings, and conceptual content — the reader now reads to discover, not just to practise. However, Stage 3 requires that new material be presented through **a single, clear viewpoint**: the reader is not yet equipped for multiple competing perspectives or genuinely ambiguous moral situations. Theory of mind competence at this age includes full first-order belief tracking and increasingly fluent second-order tracking — Tier 3 readers can understand dramatic irony ("we know the treasure is in the chest, but the villain doesn't") and follow characters with meaningfully different knowledge states. Working memory has grown substantially; Tier 3 readers can sustain interest across a full chapter book with multiple named characters, subplots, and scene transitions. In the French school system, CE2 and CM1 introduce history, geography, and science as distinct subjects — the child's world is expanding rapidly, and stories can now reflect that expansion.

## Permitted Topics

- **Adventure and exploration in new settings** — historical France, natural environments (ocean, mountain, forest), invented but internally coherent fantasy worlds; new settings are now assets, not burdens
- **Mysteries requiring inference** — evidence-based problem-solving, unreliable witnesses, clues that require the reader to reason
- **Historical and cultural contexts** — *la Gaule, le Moyen Âge, la Révolution* — French school history curriculum topics are accessible and motivating for this age
- **Science and discovery** — natural phenomena, simple scientific investigation as plot mechanic; the CE2/CM1 curriculum explicitly introduces science
- **Social complexity** — friend groups with shifting alliances, in-group/out-group dynamics, the experience of being new or different; the reader can now track multiple social relationships
- **Moral choices with clear authorial framing** — a character who must choose between loyalty and honesty; the choice has consequences; the story's framing makes the "right" answer clear even if the character struggles
- **Beginning of cultural diversity** — characters from different French regions or from other cultures; the story can reference cultural difference without requiring resolution of that difference
- **Light horror and suspense** — a genuinely frightening situation that is resolved; the reader can now sustain and enjoy controlled fear

## Topics to Avoid

- **Moral ambiguity without resolution** — "was the villain right?" should still be answerable at Tier 3; the story may present complexity but must resolve it
- **Multiple simultaneous unreliable narrators** — one perspective shift is manageable; more becomes confusing
- **Adult relationship complexity** — romantic relationships among adult characters, parental infidelity, adult-world politics; the reader is not yet equipped to navigate these as story material
- **Sustained unresolved fear or dread** — age-appropriate suspense is fine; horror that leaves the reader in genuine distress without resolution is not
- **Explicit violence with realistic consequence** — action violence in a fantasy context is fine; realistic depictions of harm with suffering are not

## Narrative Types

- **Full Hero's Journey (12 stages)** — all three acts with multiple tests and helpers; the most accessible complex narrative structure for this age
- **Mystery with investigation arc** — problem, investigation, false leads, revelation; requires and rewards logical reasoning
- **Quest narrative** — retrieve an object, deliver a message, reach a place; episodic structure with accumulating obstacles
- **Ensemble story** — a group of characters with different strengths and roles tackling a shared problem; requires tracking multiple characters (working memory now supports this)
- **Historical adventure** — period setting with child protagonist navigating a recognisable historical event; provides both narrative and educational content
- **Dual timeline** — present mystery explained by past events; requires basic temporal reasoning now available at Tier 3

## Character Archetypes

- **Protagonist with genuine inner conflict** — brave but sometimes cowardly; honest but tempted to lie; the conflict is resolved, not left open
- **Mentor with imperfect knowledge** — adult helper who doesn't have all the answers; the protagonist must act independently at key moments
- **Friend group with distinct roles** — the brave one, the cautious one, the clever one; group dynamics add social richness
- **Antagonist with understandable motivation** — not pure evil; driven by fear, greed, or misunderstanding; motivation is explained and comprehensible
- **Shape Shifter (basic)** — a character whose allegiance is initially unclear, resolved by the end
- **Up to 8 named characters** — manageable with clear role differentiation

## Emotional and Moral Range

- **Emotions accessible:** Full Tier 2 range plus: moral indignation, genuine courage tested and displayed, loyalty conflict, pride balanced with humility, empathy for an antagonist's position
- **Emotions to avoid:** Sustained unresolved shame, nihilism, sexual tension, adult grief without resolution
- **Moral complexity:** A character can make a wrong choice for good reasons; consequences are real; the story's framing provides clear moral orientation even if the situation was complex
- **Taboo topics:** Sexual content, graphic violence, adult addiction or abuse as central unresolved theme

## French Cultural Considerations

- **French school curriculum alignment:** CE2/CM1 introduces *la Gaule, les Gaulois, Charlemagne, la Révolution Française* — historical adventures set in these contexts are curriculum-resonant
- **Regional France:** Stories can now introduce recognisably different French regions (*la Bretagne, la Provence, les Alpes*) without requiring explanation — Tier 3 readers have enough geographical context
- **Francophone world:** First exposure to French-speaking Africa, Quebec, or the Caribbean as story settings is appropriate — opens the world without requiring deep cultural knowledge
- **French literary heritage:** *Les Fables* (La Fontaine), *Le Petit Prince* (Saint-Exupéry themes, not text) enter cultural consciousness at this age; stories can reference these without requiring the reader to have read them
- **School pressures:** CM1 brings first formal evaluations; stories that acknowledge academic pressure, competition, and success/failure resonate
