## Tier Writing Profile

Tier 3 readers (ages 8–10, CE2 and CM1) have reached Chall's Stage 3: reading to learn. For the first time in a child's reading development, encountering **new** information through text is not only possible but motivating — the reader now reads to discover, not just to practise fluency. This is a transformative shift: Tier 3 stories can introduce new settings, new vocabulary, and new conceptual content without competing with the decoding process. However, Stage 3 has a strong constraint: new material must be presented through **a single, clear viewpoint**. Multiple competing perspectives, deliberately ambiguous narrators, or genuine moral relativism are beyond the Stage 3 reader. Working memory is now approaching adolescent capacity (~5–6 chunks), enabling complex multi-character tracking, sustained subplots across chapters, and sentences with two levels of subordination. Theory of Mind at this age is increasingly fluent across its three AToM factors: Tier 3 readers handle dramatic irony reliably, track characters with substantially different knowledge states, and begin to appreciate ambiguity in character motivation when it is eventually resolved. CE2 and CM1 introduce formal history, geography, and science as distinct subjects — the child's sense of the world is expanding rapidly, and Tier 3 stories can reflect and extend that expansion.

## Sentence Structure

| Dimension | Guideline | Rationale |
|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| Average sentence length | 12–18 words | Sits within the 5–6 chunk range; supports more complex clause structure |
| Maximum sentence length | 28 words | Longer sentences require holding too many clause boundaries simultaneously |
| Subordinate clause nesting | Max 2 levels | Two levels (`quand X, parce que Y`) are now manageable; three levels are not |
| Preferred construction | SVO with subordinate and relative clauses | Full subordination available; participial phrases accessible |
| Participial phrases | Permitted | `Arrivant au sommet, il aperçut la vallée.` — introduces condensed information gracefully |
| Free indirect speech | Accessible | Brief interior voice without attribution marker; up to 3–4 consecutive sentences |

**Permitted constructions:**
- `Parce qu'il avait peur, il choisit de rester caché.` — causal subordinate with compound main clause
- `C'était la première fois qu'il voyait quelque chose d'aussi étrange.` — complex presentative + relative
- `Elle ne savait pas ce qui allait arriver, mais elle sentait que quelque chose avait changé.` — coordinated `que`-complements
- Participial openers: `Épuisé par la course, il s'effondra sur le sol.`
- `Il se demandait si cela avait vraiment eu lieu.` — embedded indirect question

**Prohibited constructions:**
- Triple embedding: *`Elle pensa que si elle savait que Leo croyait que...`*
- Subjunctive in complex embedded clauses (accessible in oral French, but parsing cost in reading at this tier is high): confine subjunctive to standard formulaic uses (`Il faut qu'il parte`, `Bien qu'il soit fatigué`)
- Appositive strings: *`Léa, fille courageuse, ancienne élève de CM1, chef du groupe...`*

```yaml
    - abstract political or sociological terms (démocratie, colonialisme) without narrative grounding
    - very low-frequency G3-5 words when a higher-frequency synonym exists
  favour:
    - G2 → G3-5 transition vocabulary: words familiar in oral French now encountered in reading
    - historical and geographical vocabulary aligned with CE2/CM1 curriculum (Gaulois, Révolution, région)
    - psychological and internal-state vocabulary: douter, redouter, espérer, hésiter, se méfier
    - formal connectors that build argument: cependant, néanmoins, en revanche, pourtant
```

A "new word" at Tier 3 is any word outside the reader's reading vocabulary (distinct from oral vocabulary). Tier 3 readers can infer meaning from context — this inference is the Stage 3 vocabulary acquisition mechanism. Five new words per page assumes that each new word has enough surrounding context to be inferrable. If a word is both new and non-inferrable, it counts doubly against the limit. Curriculum-aligned historical vocabulary (*Gaulois, seigneur, serf*) is treated as semi-new: the school context provides a partial schema that reduces acquisition cost.

## Narrative Pacing

- **Chapter length:** 1,200–2,000 words. Tier 3 readers sustain multi-session engagement; a chapter is not required to be completeable in a single sitting. Chapters may have 2–3 scenes with transitions.
- **Full story length:** 15,000–30,000 words (equivalent to a middle-grade novel, lower end). At this range, a Tier 3 reader can follow an extended narrative across days or weeks without losing the thread.
- **Beats per scene:** 2–4. A scene can contain: setup, complication, protagonist response, and a partial or ambiguous resolution. Not every scene ends with clean resolution.
- **Cliffhangers:** Yes, at chapter endings, and more sophisticated than Tier 2. A Tier 3 cliffhanger can be informational (`Et soudain, elle comprit.`) or character-based (`Ce n'était pas Leo. C'était quelqu'un d'autre.`) — not only action-based.
- **White space:** Paragraphs of 4–7 sentences. Scene-within-chapter transitions use a visual break. Tier 3 can sustain longer descriptive or atmospheric passages without losing the reader — but no more than 50% of any scene.
- **Exposition ratio:** Maximum 50% of any scene can be atmospheric, descriptive, or reflective — but only if it is doing character work (revealing interior state) or world-building that directly serves the plot.
- **Subplot pacing:** Tier 3 can carry 1 active subplot (secondary character arc or secondary mystery). The subplot must have its own distinct beat structure and not be ignored for more than 3 chapters.
- **Dual timeline:** If using past and present alternating chapters, the temporal shift must be clearly marked (visual separator + explicit time anchor in the first sentence). Keep each timeline strand's chapter length consistent.

## Grammar Rules and Constraints

- **Tense:** Passé simple now usable as the primary literary narrative tense — it is the canonical French literary past tense and is introduced in the CE2/CM1 curriculum. Passé simple for completed narrative actions; imparfait for states, descriptions, and ongoing processes. Passé composé remains appropriate in dialogue. Alternating passé simple (action) + imparfait (atmosphere) is the standard French literary combination at this level.
- **Passive voice:** Usable with purpose. The passive is appropriate when the agent is unknown (`La lettre avait été écrite en secret.`) or when the action's recipient deserves focus (`Il fut arrêté à l'aube.`). Do not use to avoid agent attribution where the agent matters.
- **Negation:** All standard negation forms accessible. Negative inversion in formal dialogue: `N'avez-vous pas vu...?` — appropriate for an adult character speaking formally.
- **Subjunctive:** Accessible in formulaic and near-formulaic contexts (`Il fallait qu'elle parte`, `Bien qu'il fût fatigué` — note passé du subjonctif is Stage 4+). Standard present subjunctive in dependent clauses after verbs of wanting, fearing, necessity.
- **Register:** Tier 3 stories can support multiple registers in dialogue — a peasant character speaks differently from a noble in a historical story; a child speaks differently with friends vs. teachers. Register contrast is now a character-building technique, not a confusion risk.
- **Free indirect speech:** Accessible and literarily powerful. Up to a paragraph of interior experience without `il pensa que` attribution marker. The reader is expected to track the perspective shift.

## Engagement Techniques

- **Direct address:** At choice points only. Between choice points, 3rd person or close 3rd person narration maintains immersion. The narrator does not editorialize directly to the reader outside of marked choice-point contexts.
- **Dramatic irony:** Fully accessible and powerfully engaging. Let the reader know something the protagonist does not. The gap between reader knowledge and character knowledge creates sustained motivated reading.
- **Foreshadowing:** Multi-step foreshadowing across chapters is now reliable. Plant a detail; pay it off 3–5 chapters later. The reader can hold this anticipation without losing it.
- **Humour type:** Irony, situational comedy, and character-based humour all work. A character can be funny because of their blind spots (dramatic irony). Wit in dialogue is accessible when it does not require sophisticated cultural reference.
- **Tension mechanics:** Tension may be sustained across multiple chapters with periodic relief beats. The reader at Tier 3 can tolerate unresolved tension for 3–5 chapters before needing either escalation or release.
- **World-building reveals:** New information about a setting or historical period can be delivered as narrative discovery — the protagonist learns something and the reader learns with them. This is the Stage 3 reading-to-learn mechanism put to story use.
- **Chapter hooks:** More sophisticated than Tier 2. A hook can be a revelation, a reversal, an unanswered question, or a character action with unclear motivation. All are now effective.

## Interactive Choice Mechanics

> [!pipeline]
> **Tier 3 interactive choice specification:**
>
> - **Choice point frequency:** Once every 2 chapters (approximately every 3,000–4,000 words). At Tier 3's longer chapter length, this spacing provides meaningful agency without fragmenting narrative immersion. Choices should coincide with genuine narrative decision points in the plot.
> - **Number of choices per point:** 3. The reader's working memory can now hold three options without confusion. All three should be clearly differentiated and reflect genuinely different character stances (brave/cautious/clever or similar triadic contrasts).
> - **Choice framing language (French templates):**
>   - `C'est le moment de choisir. Qu'est-ce que [protagonist] va faire ?`
>   - `[Protagonist] hésite. Et toi, que déciderais-tu à sa place ?`
>   - `Il y a trois chemins devant [protagonist]. Lequel vas-tu choisir ?`
> - **Voice at choice points:** The narrator addresses the reader directly but in a tone consistent with the story's register — literary, not game-like. The choice point should feel like the narrator is genuinely handing control over, not breaking the fourth wall abruptly.
> - **Choice consequence weight:** Moderate to significant. The choice changes the next 2–4 chapters (different characters encountered, different plot paths) before the story rejoins at a major structural beat (e.g., before the climax). At Tier 3, readers can hold the memory of their choice and appreciate consequences that last more than a scene.
> - **Prohibited choice types:**
>   - Choices that require knowledge the reader may not have (historical facts, scientific knowledge presented as prerequisite)
>   - Choices where one option is clearly narratively inferior in a way that creates retroactive distress ("you chose wrong — now Leo will suffer")
>   - Choices that split the narrative so completely that readers on different paths encounter no overlap — divergence should converge at a shared structural beat

## What to Avoid

- **Passé simple fatigue:** Passé simple is the correct literary tense but is still being consolidated at CE2/CM1. Do not write extended action sequences in passé simple with multiple irregular verbs in close succession — alternate with imparfait or présent de narration (dramatic present) for relief.
- **Unmotivated perspective shifts:** Even though free indirect speech is accessible, shifting between multiple characters' interior states within a single chapter without clear demarcation confuses Tier 3 readers. Commit to one primary perspective per chapter; pivot only at chapter breaks.
- **Simultaneous subplots at equal intensity:** One active subplot is supportable; two running at equal narrative intensity overwhelm the reader's tracking capacity. Keep a subplot clearly subordinate to the main arc.
- **Historical vocabulary dumps:** When a story is set in a period setting, resist the impulse to introduce many period-specific nouns in a single scene. Introduce one or two per scene, narratively embedded, and rely on the reader's curriculum knowledge for the rest.
- **Ironic or unreliable narrators:** A narrator who is systematically misleading the reader requires meta-level comprehension (awareness of the narrative construct) that is not yet reliable at Tier 3. Reserve for Tier 4+.
- **Extended philosophical reflection:** Tier 3 can sustain 1–2 sentences of a character thinking through a dilemma; a full chapter of interior philosophical musing loses the reader.

## Open Questions / Research Gaps

> [!question] What narrative structures work specifically in the French CM1 classroom context? The primary sources consulted (Chall, Hero's Journey) are Anglophone. French school reading at CM1 emphasises different genres and structures (les contes, le roman historique). Whether the Hero's Journey maps cleanly onto the dominant French primary literature tradition needs French-language research.

> [!question] At what precise developmental point does passé simple become narratively invisible (processed as automatised past tense) for French readers? CE2 is when it is introduced formally, but automatisation likely varies. If passé simple is still visibly "hard" at CE2, it should not dominate action sequences until CM1.

> [!question] How much prior cultural/historical knowledge can a CM1 reader be assumed to have? The curriculum introduces *la Gaule* in CE2 and *le Moyen Âge* in CM1, but school-to-school variation is significant. Stories should not assume knowledge the reader may not yet have acquired.
