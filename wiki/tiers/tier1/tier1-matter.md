## Tier Overview

Tier 1 readers (ages 6–7, CP and early CE1) are in Chall's Stage 1 of reading development: they are learning to decode. The act of translating letters into sounds consumes the majority of their cognitive resources, leaving little working memory for comprehension, character tracking, or thematic inference. Theory of mind research confirms that most 6-year-olds are approaching — but have not yet reliably crossed — the recursive mental state milestone (age ~7): they understand that characters have feelings and simple motivations, but cannot reliably track deceptive beliefs, hidden agendas, or second-order mental states ("she thinks he thinks…"). This means Tier 1 stories must be built around transparent, action-driven characters, concrete goals, and fully resolved narratives. The child's social-emotional world at this age is centred on family, home, school, and immediate peers — the first year of CP is a major transition into formal schooling (*la rentrée*), and stories that mirror this context resonate deeply. Despite cognitive constraints, children at this age are emotionally rich and imaginatively active; they enjoy wonder, silliness, animals, magic, and satisfying resolution.

## Permitted Topics

- **Everyday home and family life** — mealtimes, bedtime, getting dressed, playing in the garden; resonates with lived experience and requires no new conceptual load
- **School and classroom** — *la rentrée*, learning to read, making friends, the *cantine* (school canteen), *la récré* (recess); highly topical for Tier 1 timing
- **Animals (real and fantastical)** — pets, farm animals, forest creatures; children this age have strong emotional connections to animals and project onto them easily
- **Simple nature** — seasons, rain, the garden, the park; familiar, sensory, grounded
- **Simple friendship** — meeting a new friend, sharing, helping; social navigation is central to this age's emotional landscape
- **Short, local adventures** — going to the market (*le marché*), visiting grandparents, a day at the beach; familiar adult contexts with a child's eye
- **Gentle fantasy and magic** — a talking animal, a small magical object, a wish that comes true; must be bounded and resolved, not open-ended
- **Humour and silliness** — wordplay (simple), absurd situations, comic misunderstandings; Tier 1 readers love to laugh

## Topics to Avoid

- **Unfamiliar or complex settings** — exotic locations requiring background knowledge (outer space, medieval castles, jungle) add cognitive burden that competes with decoding; if used, must be immediately and visually grounded
- **Death and grief** — too emotionally heavy for an unresolved narrative; may be touched tangentially (a plant that wilts and recovers) but not as a central theme
- **Violence or physical harm with consequence** — a character getting hurt in ways that require emotional processing rather than comic treatment
- **Complex family structures requiring explanation** — divorce, illness, estrangement; not because they don't exist in children's lives, but because the story must resolve them, and that requires nuance Tier 1 doesn't yet have the reading tools to access
- **Abstract moral questions without resolution** — "was it right to lie?" requires reflective moral reasoning beyond this tier
- **New informational content as the story's purpose** — Chall Stage 2 research confirms: Tier 1 and 2 readers build fluency on familiar content; informational novelty competes with decoding
- **Antagonists with complex hidden agendas** — Theory of Mind constraint: characters can be mean or unhelpful, but their internal reasoning must be transparent or irrelevant

## Narrative Types

- **Simple problem–solution arc** — the most accessible: a problem arises, the protagonist tries things, the problem is solved; satisfying and low-demand
- **Circular story** — the story begins and ends in the same place (*chez soi*); the journey away and return provides structure and reassurance
- **Accumulative/chain story** — each scene adds one new element (e.g., bringing in more animals, more characters, more objects); builds pattern recognition and vocabulary repetition
- **Quest with a single goal** — go somewhere, find something, bring it back; Proppian minimal arc (Absentation → Departure → Return)
- **Day-in-the-life** — no external problem; instead, a warm sequence of small moments with a satisfying ending; appropriate for mood-based stories

## Character Archetypes

- **Child or animal protagonist** — self-projection for the reader; should have one clear dominant trait (curious, clumsy, kind, brave) rather than complex inner conflict
- **Helpful adult figure (parent, teacher, grandparent)** — fulfils the Mentor role but should not be the one who solves the problem; the protagonist must take action
- **Animal companion** — a pet or friend who helps, listens, or follows the protagonist; emotionally resonant and cognitively easy to track
- **Simple obstacle figure** — a character who blocks the goal (not from malice but from indifference, busyness, or simple opposition); must be explicitly non-threatening; resolution through the protagonist's resourcefulness, not defeat
- **Trickster element (optional)** — a funny, surprising character who adds comic relief; must not confuse the protagonist's goal

**Character count limit:** Maximum 3 named characters. The reader cannot track more than a few characters at a time (working memory constraint).

## Emotional and Moral Range

- **Emotions accessible:** Joy, excitement, fear (mild, quickly resolved), sadness (with resolution), surprise, pride, curiosity, love, silliness
- **Emotions to avoid:** Sustained dread, grief without resolution, jealousy that is not resolved, rage, shame
- **Moral complexity:** Simple binary. Right vs. wrong is clear. Characters make mistakes but recognise them. No ambiguity about who is good or bad.
- **Taboo topics:** Death as a central unresolved theme; sexual content; violence with realistic consequence; parental conflict; poverty framed as permanent

> [!pipeline]
> Tier 1 emotional contract: every story must end with the protagonist in a better or restored state than they started. Unresolved sadness or fear is not appropriate at this tier.

## French Cultural Considerations

- **La rentrée scolaire** (September school start) is the defining cultural event for CP — stories set at the start of school are highly resonant for Tier 1
- **La cantine** (school canteen), **la récréation** (recess), **le cartable** (schoolbag), **la maîtresse/le maître** (teacher) are universal cultural touchstones at this age
- **Le marché, la boulangerie, la ferme** — familiar neighbourhood/rural spaces that appear frequently in G1 French school readers (per MANULEX corpus)
- **Les grandes vacances** (summer holidays) and **Noël** are the two major temporal anchors; stories can be set around these without needing explanation
- **French animal culture:** *la vache, le lapin, la poule, le renard* (cow, rabbit, hen, fox) are canonical French children's story animals — favour over exotic animals
- **Avoid:** Anglophone pop culture references (Disney, Marvel), American settings or idioms, vocabulary borrowed from English without French anchoring
