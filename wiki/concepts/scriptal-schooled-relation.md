## Definition

The *scriptal-schooled relation to language* (French: *rapport scriptural-scolaire au langage*) is a concept developed by sociologist [[Bernard Lahire]] to describe the distinctive, formal, abstract, and reflexive relationship to language that French schooling constructs in children.

In this mode, language is treated as an **object of analysis** rather than a tool of everyday communication:
- You think about grammar rules, not just whether a sentence sounds right
- You write to be evaluated by an absent reader, not to communicate with someone present
- You reflect on language *as* language, stepping back from its immediate use

This contrasts with *raison pratique* (practical reason) — the everyday, embodied, contextual use of language that most children bring from home.

## The "Forme Scolaire" (School Form)

The scriptal-schooled relation is instantiated through the [[Forme Scolaire]] — the historically specific set of school practices that organize learning: individual written tasks, decontextualized exercises, graded assessment, silent reading, standardized language norms. The forme scolaire is not neutral — it inherently advantages children who have already encountered this register at home.

## Relationship to Inequality

Lahire uses this concept to extend [[Pierre Bourdieu]]'s theory of cultural capital:
- Bourdieu argued that middle-class children succeed in school because they inherit the "right" cultural capital at home
- Lahire argues that schooling itself — through the forme scolaire — actively *constructs* an abstract relation to language that excludes children unfamiliar with it
- This is a production of inequality, not just its reproduction

This matters particularly in France, where the [[Jules Ferry]] reforms (late 19th century) established universal compulsory schooling that embedded these formal linguistic expectations for all children, regardless of background.

## Significance to the Project

> [!tip] Actionable for our project
> A children's book that reads like a school exercise (formal, analytical, distanced) will trigger the *scriptal-schooled* mode and lose readers. Our book should deliberately operate in *practical reason* mode: direct address, embodied situations, characters whose language feels spoken rather than written, stories grounded in action and sensory detail rather than abstract reflection.

> [!tip]
> The scriptal-schooled relation also explains why French children may feel that "reading" is a school duty rather than a pleasure. A book that signals it is *not* school — through informal register, humor, adventure, or surprise — can be liberating.
