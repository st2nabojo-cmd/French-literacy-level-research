## Definition

Theory of Mind (ToM) is the cognitive capacity to attribute mental states — beliefs, desires, intentions, knowledge, emotions — to oneself and others, and to understand that other people's mental states may differ from one's own and from reality. Advanced Theory of Mind (AToM) refers specifically to the more sophisticated capacities that develop during middle childhood (ages 5–10), beyond the basic false-belief understanding typically achieved by age 4.

The key developmental research (from [[2026-05-07-theory-of-mind-middle-childhood]]) identifies three distinct AToM factors that develop progressively between ages 5 and 10:

1. **Social and Cognitive Reasoning** — understanding social inference, complex deception, and second/third-order mental state reasoning ("she thinks he thinks...")
2. **Reasoning About Ambiguity** — understanding that situations can be genuinely ambiguous, that people may draw different valid conclusions from the same evidence, and that a character's true motivation may be unknowable
3. **Naturalistic Social Interpretation** — applying ToM fluidly to natural, unstructured social situations (not just explicit tests), including understanding sarcasm, irony, and polite deception

The critical milestone for Nabojo story content is the **AToM milestone at approximately age 7**: the point at which children reliably understand that a character can hold a false belief, enabling simple secrets, surprises, and deception in stories. Before this milestone, character motivations must be transparent. After it, hidden agendas, dramatic irony, and simple deception become accessible and engaging.

## Why This Matters for Nabojo

Theory of Mind directly governs what kinds of character behaviour and narrative construction are comprehensible and engaging at each tier. A story that assumes ToM capacity the reader does not yet have will be confusing; a story that stays below the reader's actual ToM capacity will feel flat and unsurprising.

For the Matter pipeline:
- **Before AToM milestone (Tier 1):** No hidden agendas; no deception; no characters whose stated motivation differs from their true motivation. Antagonists block the protagonist through indifference or simple opposition, not through strategy or deception.
- **At AToM milestone (Tier 2):** Simple deception, secrets, and surprises become engaging. A character can know something another character doesn't. Dramatic irony (reader knows what character doesn't) begins to work.
- **With growing AToM fluency (Tier 3):** Multiple characters with substantially different knowledge states; sustained dramatic irony; ambiguous motivation that is eventually resolved.
- **With full AToM competence (Tier 4):** Complex multi-step deception; characters with genuinely uncertain motivations; dramatic irony sustained across an entire novel; the reader can tolerate leaving some motivations unresolved.

## Tier-Specific Notes

### Tier 1 (Ages 6–7 — Pre-milestone / At threshold)
Most Tier 1 readers are approaching but have not yet reliably crossed the AToM milestone. Character motivations must be **transparent and explicit**. If an obstacle figure is blocking the protagonist, their reason must be either stated outright or behaviourally obvious. Hidden agendas, deceptive characters, and characters whose stated intentions differ from their real ones are not yet comprehensible. Dramatic irony — where the reader knows something the character doesn't — is not yet reliable.

**What's accessible:** Characters have clearly legible feelings (happy, sad, scared, angry). Characters want things and act to get them. A character can be "mean" as a trait without requiring explanation of why they are mean.

### Tier 2 (Ages 7–8 — At/past the milestone)
The AToM milestone is crossed at approximately age 7. Tier 2 readers can now reliably understand that a character can hold a **false belief** — believe something that is not true. This enables:
- Simple secrets (`Marie didn't tell Leo the surprise was a party`)
- Surprises (the reader knew; the character didn't)
- Simple deception (a character says one thing but means another — when this is revealed, the reader understands it retrospectively)
- Basic dramatic irony (reader and narrator share knowledge the protagonist lacks)

**What's still constrained:** Second-order mental states (`she thinks he thinks...`) are emerging but not yet reliable. Sustained deception across many scenes is at the edge. Complex motivational ambiguity is not yet accessible.

### Tier 3 (Ages 8–10 — Growing fluency across all three AToM factors)
Tier 3 readers are developing fluency across all three AToM factors. Factor 1 (social reasoning) is becoming robust — second-order mental states are increasingly reliable. Factor 2 (reasoning about ambiguity) is emerging — readers can now understand that a character's motivation might be genuinely unclear, though they expect eventual resolution. Factor 3 (naturalistic interpretation) is growing — sarcasm and irony in dialogue become consistently comprehensible.

**What's accessible:** Dramatic irony sustained across multiple chapters. Multiple characters with substantially different knowledge states. An antagonist whose motivation is explained and makes comprehensible sense even if morally wrong. A Shape Shifter character whose allegiance is unclear for most of the story (resolved at the end). Sarcasm and irony in dialogue.

### Tier 4 (Ages 10–12 — Approaching full adult AToM)
All three AToM factors are near or at adult competence. Tier 4 readers can:
- Track complex multi-step deception (a character deceives another character who then deceives a third)
- Sustain genuine uncertainty about a character's motivation across an entire novel without resolution anxiety
- Understand and appreciate unreliable narrators (a narrator whose account cannot be fully trusted)
- Appreciate moral complexity where the "villain" is right about some things
- Follow dramatic irony sustained for an entire book

**The Tier 4 exclusive:** Factor 2 (ambiguity) at full competence means the reader can leave a story without every question answered. Not every character's motivation needs to be explained; not every moral question needs resolution. This is the most significant AToM-gated capability for Nabojo story design.

## What Sources Say

- [[2026-05-07-theory-of-mind-middle-childhood]]: Primary source. A longitudinal study (ages 5–10) identifying three AToM factors. Key findings: Factor 1 (social/cognitive reasoning) develops first; all three factors continue developing through age 10 and beyond; the AToM milestone at ~age 7 marks first reliable false-belief understanding across all three factors. The study finds that AToM is not a single capacity but a set of related abilities with distinct developmental trajectories.

> [!warning] The AToM research is primarily Anglophone and based on standardised test conditions. How these capacities manifest in naturalistic story engagement — where context is richer and more familiar than a test condition — may differ. A child who fails a false-belief test may still enjoy a story involving simple deception if the narrative context is sufficiently supportive.

## Open Questions

> [!question] What is the precise AToM profile of a French 7-year-old entering CE1? The research reports group-level developmental trajectories; individual variation at the 7-year threshold may be wide. A Tier 2 story that relies on the AToM milestone may be comprehensible for some CE1 readers and not others.

> [!question] How does rich narrative context support AToM-dependent story elements beyond what test conditions predict? Children may understand deception in a familiar narrative context earlier than formal tests suggest. This would imply that Tier 1 restrictions could be slightly relaxed for narrative-embedded (vs. explicit) deception.

> [!question] Does emotional engagement with characters accelerate or deepen AToM processing in story contexts? Stories may actually train AToM, not just test it. If interactive stories with character stakes accelerate AToM development, Nabojo's platform may be having a positive developmental effect — but no research specifically addresses interactive digital fiction and AToM development in young French readers.
