## Definition

Early literacy (also called *emergent literacy*, or in French *littératie émergente*) refers to the foundational reading and writing skills that develop before children learn to decode text formally. It is observed primarily in preschool and kindergarten-age children (roughly ages 3–6) and is the strongest predictor of later reading success.

In the French research tradition, four core domains are currently recognized:

| Domain | French term | What it involves |
|--------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Letter Knowledge** | Connaissance des lettres | Recognizing and naming letters; understanding letter-sound correspondences |
| **Vocabulary** | Vocabulaire | Breadth and depth of oral vocabulary |
| **Phonological Awareness** | Conscience phonologique | Sensitivity to sounds, syllables, and phonemes; ability to manipulate them |
| **Oral Comprehension** | Compréhension orale | Understanding of spoken stories and discourse |

## Significance to the Project

While our target age group (8–12) is well past the *emergent* literacy stage, understanding these four foundational domains clarifies what proficiency French children arrive with when they begin reading independently. Specifically:

- **Vocabulary** remains a critical variable at ages 8–12 — books need to expand vocabulary without overwhelming readers
- **Phonological awareness** explains why rhyme, rhythm, and wordplay are so powerful in children's literature (even for fluent readers)
- **Oral comprehension** is the bridge between listening to stories and reading them — relevant if our book is ever used in read-aloud contexts

> [!tip] Actionable for our project
> The four early literacy domains are the building blocks of fluent reading. A children's book that activates all four — rich vocabulary in context, phonological play through rhythm/rhyme, clear oral-style prose, and engaging narrative comprehension — is pedagogically sound *and* more enjoyable.
