## Definition

Literacy inequality in France refers to the systematic differences in reading and writing abilities — and in access to the cultural benefits literacy provides — that correlate with children's socioeconomic status, class background, and family literacy environment.

This is not merely a natural variation in ability. Multiple French scholars argue it is *produced* by the structure of French schooling itself.

## Two Layers of Inequality

### 1. Inherited Cultural Capital (Bourdieu's framework)
[[Pierre Bourdieu]] and [[Jean-Claude Passeron]] argued that middle- and upper-class children succeed in school because they arrive with *cultural capital* — familiarity with formal language, books, academic vocabulary — that lower-class children lack. School rewards what was already acquired at home.

### 2. School-Produced Inequality (Lahire's extension)
[[Bernard Lahire]] argues that Bourdieu's account is incomplete. Schooling itself actively produces a distinctive [[Scriptal-Schooled Relation]] to language. Children who cannot adopt this abstract, reflexive, decontextualized mode of engaging with language are systematically disadvantaged — not just because they lack inherited capital, but because the school *form* itself is alienating to them.

### 3. Historical Construction of Failure (Chartier's contribution)
[[Anne-Marie Chartier]] shows that compulsory reading — mandated by the state — paradoxically created new categories of failure. When reading became obligatory, those who struggled became visible as "failures" in a way they hadn't been before.

## French-Specific Context

- The [[Jules Ferry]] reforms (1880s) established free, secular, compulsory schooling in France — creating the infrastructure through which literacy was universalized but also through which failure became institutionalized
- The highly codified nature of French (complex orthography, strict grammar norms, prestige register) makes the gap between home and school language particularly sharp for many families
- The [[EPLE Scale]] research ([[2026-04-22-eple-french-literacy-scale]]) confirms that SES is a significant predictor of early literacy performance in French preschoolers

## Significance to the Project

> [!tip] Actionable for our project
> If our book aims to reach a broad range of French 8–12-year-olds, it should be designed to be accessible to children with lower SES backgrounds — using clear language, rich but contextually grounded vocabulary, and narrative structures that don't presuppose cultural capital. This is both ethically sound and commercially smart (broader audience).
