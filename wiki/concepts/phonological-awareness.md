## Definition

Phonological awareness is the ability to detect, identify, and manipulate the sound structure of language — including words, syllables, and individual phonemes — independently of meaning. It is one of the four core domains of [[Early Literacy]] and is one of the strongest predictors of reading acquisition.

Examples of phonological awareness tasks:
- Rhyme detection ("do *chat* and *rat* rhyme?")
- Syllable segmentation ("how many syllables in *maison*?")
- Phoneme isolation ("what is the first sound in *fleuve*?")
- Phoneme blending ("/f/ /l/ /ø/ /v/ — what word?")

## French-Specific Considerations

French has phonological characteristics that differ significantly from English, making English-language phonological assessment tools inappropriate for French learners:
- French has **nasal vowels** (/ã/, /ɛ̃/, /ɔ̃/) not present in English
- French liaison (linking of final consonants across word boundaries) creates complex phonological environments
- French syllable structure is more open (CV) than English (which allows complex clusters)
- French has many **homophones** (e.g., *mer*, *mère*, *maire*) — phonological sensitivity is essential for orthographic accuracy

This is one reason why the [[EPLE Scale]] was needed: phonological assessment in French requires purpose-built tools.

## Significance to the Project

Phonological awareness is not just a preschool concern — it underlies reading fluency, spelling, and word recognition throughout childhood. For our book project (ages 8–12):

- **Rhyme and rhythm** activate phonological awareness even in fluent readers, making text more memorable and enjoyable
- **Wordplay** (puns, alliteration, tongue twisters) is deeply rooted in phonological sensitivity and is a hallmark of quality French children's literature
- French has a rich tradition of sound-play in children's verse (comptines), which our book can draw on

> [!tip] Actionable for our project
> Incorporating rhythmic prose, alliteration, or phonological wordplay is not just aesthetically pleasing — it taps into a deep linguistic competence that children enjoy exercising. French children are particularly attuned to sound patterns given French's distinctive phonology.
