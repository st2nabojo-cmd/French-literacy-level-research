## Definition

Chall's Stages of Reading Development (Jeanne Chall, 1983) is a developmental model describing how reading ability progresses from pre-literacy through adult expert reading across six stages (Stage 0–5). Each stage has a distinct cognitive profile: different demands on working memory, different relationships between decoding and comprehension, and different roles for prior knowledge. The model is the foundational framework for matching story complexity to reader capability. For Nabojo, the four stages spanning ages 6–12 (Stages 1–3, with Stage 4 beginning) are directly operationalised in the four literacy tiers.

The critical non-obvious insight from Chall is the **Stage 2 paradox**: children who have become somewhat fluent (ages 7–8) are *not* ready for informational novelty. Stage 2 reading is for confirming familiar content, not for learning new content. This directly constrains Tier 2 Matter (familiar settings only) and Manner (no new vocabulary domains).

## Why This Matters for Nabojo

Chall's stages are the primary constraint engine for the Manner tier pages. Every sentence length, vocabulary ceiling, and exposition ratio decision in the Manner pages is calibrated against the cognitive profile of the reader's Chall stage. Without Chall, the tier system has no principled basis for its writing constraints.

For the pipeline:
- **Matter stage:** Chall's Stage 2 paradox directly constrains what topics are permitted at Tier 2 (familiar settings only, no new information as story engine)
- **Manner stage:** Sentence complexity, subordination level, tense usage, and vocabulary newness are all stage-gated

## Tier-Specific Notes

### Tier 1 (Stage 1 — Ages 6–7)
All cognitive resources are allocated to decoding. There is no spare capacity for comprehension of complex sentences, novel vocabulary, or inferential content. The primary writing constraint: maximum simplicity in every formal dimension (sentence length, vocabulary, syntax). Content must be emotionally engaging while being formally minimal.

### Tier 2 (Stage 2 — Ages 7–8)
The Stage 2 paradox: fluency is being built, but it is built on **familiar** content. Introducing new information (new settings, new vocabulary domains, new conceptual content) directly competes with the fluency-building mechanism. Tier 2 stories may extend narrative complexity (more characters, more plot structure) but must keep **settings and concepts** within the reader's prior knowledge. This is the most commonly misunderstood stage — "they can read, so they can learn through reading" is wrong at Stage 2.

### Tier 3 (Stage 3 — Ages 8–10)
Reading to learn is now the primary function. New information, new settings, and new vocabulary domains are accessible and motivating — Stage 3 is when reading genuinely expands a child's world. However, Stage 3 has a single-viewpoint constraint: new material must be presented through a single, clear perspective. Multiple competing voices, moral relativism, and deliberately ambiguous narrators exceed Stage 3 processing capacity.

### Tier 4 (Approaching Stage 4 — Ages 10–12)
Stage 4 is characterised by the beginning of multi-viewpoint processing: the reader can hold and evaluate multiple competing perspectives simultaneously. This is still nascent at ages 10–12 — the full Stage 4 capacity is achieved in adolescence. Tier 4 writing can introduce genuine moral ambiguity, unreliable narrators (handled carefully), and complex character motivations, but should not assume the reader has fully consolidated Stage 4 capacity.

## What Sources Say

- [[2026-05-07-chall-stages-reading-development]]: Primary source. Defines all 5 stages with age ranges and cognitive profiles. Confirms the Stage 2 paradox: "the reading of Stages 1 and 2 serves mainly to confirm what is already known." Confirms Stage 3 as the reading-to-learn threshold. Notes that Stage 4 (multiple viewpoints) begins in adolescence.

> [!warning] Chall's stages were developed from English-language reading research. French orthographic opacity (more irregular grapheme-phoneme correspondence than English) may mean that French children reach Stage 2 automaticity later than their English-speaking counterparts. The Stage 1/2 boundary in French may be CE1 into CE2 rather than CP into CE1. No French-language validation of Chall's stage boundaries was found.

## Open Questions

> [!question] Do Chall's stage boundaries apply to French orthography? French is more orthographically opaque than English — a French child may still be substantially decoding at the age an English child has reached Stage 2 automaticity. French-specific literacy stage research would sharpen all four Manner tier pages.

> [!question] How does Chall Stage 3's "single viewpoint" constraint interact with close third-person narration? A close third that occasionally dips into a secondary character's interior for one sentence — is this a Stage 3 violation? No data was found.
