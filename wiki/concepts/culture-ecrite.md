## Definition

*Culture écrite* (written culture) is the term preferred by French scholars in place of the Anglo-American concept of *literacy*. The distinction is not merely terminological — it reflects a fundamentally different approach to understanding reading and writing.

| *Literacy* (Anglo-American) | *Culture écrite* (French) |
|---|---|
| Focuses on individual cognitive skills | Focuses on social and cultural practices |
| Tends toward universal, neutral framing | Emphasizes historical and situated specificity |
| Measurable competency (can you read?) | Participatory membership (do you live in a written-culture world?) |
| Used in education policy and assessment | Used in sociology, history, anthropology |

## Why the Term "Literacy" is Controversial in France

The word *literacy* was imported into French as *littéracie* (a neologism) and has not been fully accepted. French scholars tend to resist the concept because:
1. It implies a **deficit model** — you either have literacy or you don't
2. It is associated with **Anglo-American assessment regimes** (PISA, PIRLS) that French scholars view with suspicion
3. French intellectual tradition prefers to see reading and writing as embedded in **historical and social practices**, not as decontextualized cognitive skills

## Significance to the Project

> [!info] Contextual note
> Understanding this tension helps explain why French children's literacy research can be hard to locate using standard English search terms. Searching for "littéracie" or "culture écrite" in French databases may yield different (and complementary) results to searches for "literacy" in English databases.

> [!tip] Actionable for our project
> A French children's book that invites children into the *culture of writing* — as a world of stories, ideas, and imagination — rather than presenting itself as a vehicle for developing a skill (*literacy*) may be better received by French educators and parents. The framing matters.
