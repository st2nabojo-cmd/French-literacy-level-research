## Definition

Working memory is the cognitive system responsible for temporarily holding and manipulating information during active processing. In reading, working memory determines how much of a sentence a reader can hold in mind while processing its end — and therefore, how complex a sentence they can reliably comprehend.

**Chunking** is the mechanism by which working memory is extended: coherent units of meaning (words, phrases, sentences) are bound into a single "chunk" and handled as one cognitive object, freeing up capacity for additional chunks. A fluent reader processes a familiar word as 1 chunk; a decoding reader processes each letter separately, potentially consuming 4–6 chunks on a single word.

The key pipeline finding from research: **a coherent sentence processed as a meaning unit counts as approximately 1 chunk**, regardless of sentence length — but only if the sentence is within the reader's fluency range. A sentence that exceeds the reader's parsing capacity breaks into multiple demanding sub-units, consuming more chunks than available.

**Working memory capacity by age (approximate, from chunking research):**
| Age | Chunk capacity | Tier |
|-----|---------------|------|
| 6–7 | ~3–4 chunks | Tier 1 |
| 7–8 | ~4–5 chunks | Tier 2 |
| 8–10 | ~5–6 chunks | Tier 3 |
| 10–12 | ~6–7 chunks | Tier 4 |
| Adult | ~7 chunks | — |

## Why This Matters for Nabojo

Working memory capacity directly determines sentence complexity ceilings for each tier. Every sentence length guideline, subordination limit, and clause nesting restriction in the Manner tier pages is grounded in the chunking capacity of the target age group. Without this framework, sentence complexity constraints would be arbitrary editorial preferences rather than principled developmental constraints.

For the pipeline:
- A sentence that fits within the chunk budget for a given tier is processed as narrative content; one that exceeds it is processed as a decoding problem that blocks comprehension
- The maximum sentence length caps in each Manner tier page (12 words at Tier 1, 20 at Tier 2, 28 at Tier 3, 40 at Tier 4) are calibrated to leave at least 1–2 chunks free for the reader's tracking of narrative context (characters, setting, prior events)
- Character count limits in Matter tier pages also derive from chunking: each named character in working memory during a scene costs approximately 1 chunk; exceeding the character limit erodes the budget available for processing the current sentence

## Tier-Specific Notes

### Tier 1 (Ages 6–7 — ~3–4 chunks)
At Stage 1, decoding is not yet automatic. Every unfamiliar word may consume 2–4 chunks on its own while the letter-sound mapping is performed. With decoding load added to the chunk budget, effective capacity for sentence meaning may drop to 1–2 chunks. This is why sentences must be extremely short (5–8 words average), syntactically minimal (SVO, no subordination), and drawn entirely from familiar vocabulary (so each word costs 1 chunk, not more).

Three named characters is the maximum because tracking three distinct character identities in memory alongside processing a sentence is the ceiling of the available budget. Any named character beyond three is likely to be lost.

### Tier 2 (Ages 7–8 — ~4–5 chunks)
Decoding is becoming automatic for high-frequency vocabulary, freeing up chunks for comprehension. The growing capacity (4–5 chunks) enables: one subordinate clause (which adds roughly 1 chunk of clause-boundary tracking), 4–5 named characters (each ~0.5–1 chunk in active working memory during a scene), and slightly longer sentences (8–13 words average, max 20 words).

The Stage 2 familiar-content constraint and the chunk capacity constraint reinforce each other: unfamiliar vocabulary in Stage 2 not only triggers inference work but also consumes chunks that would otherwise be available for sentence parsing.

### Tier 3 (Ages 8–10 — ~5–6 chunks)
Near-adolescent working memory capacity. Two levels of clause nesting are now supportable (e.g., a temporal clause containing a relative clause). Multi-character tracking scales to 8 named characters. Scenes can carry 2–3 active subplots without losing the reader's narrative thread. The freed-up chunk capacity is what enables Stage 3's "reading to learn" function — there is now capacity to both process new information AND hold the narrative context.

### Tier 4 (Ages 10–12 — ~6–7 chunks)
Near-adult capacity. Complex sentences with three levels of embedding, large cast tracking (no meaningful ceiling on named characters when well-differentiated), and extended subplots are all within budget. The remaining chunk constraint at Tier 4 is not capacity but **clarity**: even with 6–7 chunks available, a sentence that uses all of them for clause-boundary tracking leaves no capacity for the reader to connect the sentence to prior narrative context. Structural complexity must serve content density, not substitute for it.

## What Sources Say

- [[2026-05-07-chunking-memory-working-memory-development]]: Primary source. A cognitive psychology paper on working memory development across childhood. Confirms age-by-age chunk capacity growth. Establishes that coherent sentences processed as meaning units cost approximately 1 chunk each. Provides the data underlying the character count limits and sentence length caps in all four Manner tier pages.

> [!warning] Working memory capacity is reported as group averages with significant individual variation. A 7-year-old at the low end of the Tier 2 range may have chunk capacity equivalent to Tier 1, while an advanced 7-year-old may already be at Tier 3 capacity. The tier guidelines target the median for the age range; strong and weak readers will need human editorial judgment about tier placement.

## Open Questions

> [!question] How does reading modality (screen vs. paper, read-aloud vs. silent) affect effective working memory capacity in this age range? Nabojo is a screen-based e-reader; the research on chunking is from print contexts. If screen reading imposes additional cognitive load, effective chunk budgets may be lower on the Nabojo device than research suggests.

> [!question] Does Nabojo's interactive branching mechanic (choice points) impose additional working memory load? Each choice point requires the reader to hold the current story state plus evaluate multiple future options. This is a working-memory-expensive operation. Whether choice points should be placed at low-complexity narrative moments (to free up chunk budget for the decision) has not been studied.

> [!question] How quickly does chunk capacity grow within a tier? The research reports cross-sectional averages; a 10-year-old CM1 student and a 12-year-old 6ème student have meaningfully different chunk capacities despite both being Tier 4. If Nabojo subdivides tiers within CM1/CM2/6ème, a finer-grained capacity profile would be needed.
