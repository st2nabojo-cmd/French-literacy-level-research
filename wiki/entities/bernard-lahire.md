## Description

Bernard Lahire is a French sociologist, professor at the [[École Normale Supérieure]], Lettres et Sciences Humaines, in Lyon, France, where he heads a research group on socialization. A prolific scholar (over a dozen books, many translated), he is one of the leading figures in the French sociological study of literacy, schooling, and social inequality.

## Role in Research Domain

Lahire is the originator of the [[Scriptal-Schooled Relation]] concept — the idea that formal schooling constructs a distinctive, abstract, and reflexive relationship to language that goes beyond the cultural capital children inherit at home. This concept is central to understanding why French schooled literacy can exclude children who haven't internalized this formal relation to language.

He critiques and extends [[Pierre Bourdieu]] and [[Jean-Claude Passeron]]'s *La Reproduction* (1970): while Bourdieu attributed school failure primarily to unequal distribution of inherited cultural capital, Lahire argues that schooling itself actively *produces* a new form of inequality through the [[Forme Scolaire]].

## Key Works

- *La Raison Scolaire: École et Pratiques d'Écriture, Entre Savoir et Pouvoir* (2008, Presses Universitaires de Rennes) — the primary work cited in [[2026-04-22-paradoxes-french-literacy-instruction]]
- Earlier works on social inequality and written culture (1993, 1995)

## Key Claims

- Schooling constructs an "abstract reflexive relation to language" independent of home cultural capital
- The *forme scolaire* (school form) is a historically specific set of practices that make language an object of analysis, not just communication
- School failure is not just reproduced inequality — it is actively produced by the schooling system itself
- The *raison scolaire* (logic of schooling) contrasts with *raison pratique* (practical reason), which characterizes everyday language use
