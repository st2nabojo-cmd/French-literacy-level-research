## Description

Anne-Marie Chartier is a French educational historian specializing in the history of reading instruction, approached from the perspective of teachers and their everyday practice. Her major work, *L'École et la Lecture Obligatoire: Histoire et Paradoxes des Pratiques d'Enseignement de la Lecture* (Schooling and Compulsory Reading: History and Paradoxes of Reading Instruction Practices, Retz, 2007), traces reading pedagogy from the 17th century to the present.

## Role in Research Domain

Chartier's teacher-centered perspective is distinctive: rather than looking at official curricula or policy, she focuses on what teachers actually did in classrooms over centuries. This "from-the-ground-up" history reveals that literacy instruction is full of contradictions — what was officially required often diverged sharply from what teachers found workable or meaningful.

## Key Claims

- Teachers have been the primary practical agents shaping what literacy instruction actually meant day-to-day
- Reading became compulsory in France through a paradoxical process — mandating it created new forms of failure and resistance
- Numerous contradictions and paradoxes mark the history of French reading instruction (e.g., compulsory reading producing reluctance to read)
